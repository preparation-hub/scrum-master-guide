01 Your Positioning

Who you are and how you frame your role

Opening, identity-setting questions. Answer honestly, but frame yourself as someone already operating beyond pure ceremony-running.

Q1

Are you managing at a team level, or are you a designated Agile Coach?

Be honest about your title, then show the range of what you actually do. Most SAFe Scrum Masters sit between the two.

✎
Suggested framing "My designated role is Scrum Master at the team level, supporting [1–2] teams on the ART. In practice I also do coaching work — mentoring the PO on backlog health, running Agile maturity assessments, and helping other teams with dependency and flow issues. So I'm operating as a team-level SM with a coaching footprint, and moving toward a full Agile Coach role."

Key distinction to signal you know: a Scrum Master serves 1–2 teams and the process; an Agile Coach works across teams, leadership, and the organisation on the broader transformation and mindset.

02 Agile Maturity & Metrics

Assessing how mature a team really is

They want to hear that maturity is multi-dimensional and trend-based — never a single vanity number.

Q2

How do you assess the Agile maturity of a team? What metrics do you use, and how do you evaluate?

I assess maturity across several dimensions, not one score, using a mix of a structured assessment plus objective delivery data.

Dimensions I look at:

  • Delivery predictability — does the team reliably meet its Sprint Goal and PI objectives?
  • Quality & engineering practices — CI/CD, automated testing, low escaped defects, low tech debt.
  • Self-organisation — does the team plan and solve problems without me driving every decision?
  • Collaboration & transparency — healthy backlog, honest Daily Scrums, useful retros with follow-through.
  • Value focus — does the team understand the "why" and outcomes, not just output?

Metrics: say/do ratio (commitment reliability), velocity stability (not size), sprint/PI predictability, defect leakage, cycle time and flow efficiency, and team happiness/engagement. Tools like a SAFe/Agility Health self-assessment add the qualitative view.

★
Evaluation "I read trends over 3–4 sprints, not a snapshot, and I combine the numbers with a team self-assessment. A team can have high velocity and still be immature if quality or self-organisation is weak. I use it to start a coaching conversation, never to grade the team."
03 Delivery Challenges & Scenarios

Spillover, missed releases, and stakeholder pressure

Scenario questions test judgment. The winning pattern: get curious about root cause, be transparent early, protect quality, and put in proactive prevention.

Q3

How do you handle recurring spillover of user stories? What have you done to improve it?

Recurring spillover is a symptom — I treat it as a retro topic and chase the root cause rather than pushing the team harder.

Common root causes & my fixes:

  • Over-commitment → plan to capacity and historical velocity, not optimism; leave buffer for BAU/support.
  • Stories too big → stronger refinement and vertical slicing so items finish within a sprint.
  • Weak Definition of Ready → don't pull a story into the sprint until it's clear, estimated, and dependency-free.
  • Mid-sprint scope / interruptions → protect the Sprint Goal, track unplanned work, make it visible.
  • Hidden dependencies → surface and resolve them in planning and Scrum of Scrums.
★
What I did "On my team, chronic spillover dropped after we enforced a Definition of Ready and split large stories. Our say/do ratio went from ~65% to consistently above 85% over three sprints."
Q4

A critical feature misses a SAFe release. How do you handle it — immediate actions and proactive measures?

Immediate actions (transparency first):

  1. Raise it early — the moment the risk is real, not at the deadline. Inform the PO, RTE and stakeholders.
  2. Assess impact honestly — what's done, what's not, and the options.
  3. Explore alternatives: descope to an MVP, ship behind a feature toggle, or deliver a partial slice that still gives value.
  4. Coordinate at ART level via the RTE for cross-team dependencies; re-plan the remainder into the next iteration/PI.
  5. Run a quick root-cause review so the miss becomes a learning, not a blame exercise.

Proactive measures to prevent recurrence:

  • Define MVP vs nice-to-have up front so there's always a shippable core.
  • Realistic PI planning with capacity buffers and uncommitted (stretch) objectives.
  • Actively manage dependencies and ROAM program risks.
  • Continuous integration and incremental delivery — integrate every sprint, not at the end.
  • Feature flags so incomplete work can ship dark and be released when ready.
Q5

How would you protect your team from stakeholder pressure?

  • Single channel: route all work through the Product Owner and the backlog — no side-assignments to developers.
  • Use data, not opinions: show capacity and velocity so "can we add this?" becomes a visible trade-off conversation.
  • Protect the Sprint Goal: new asks go to the backlog for the next sprint, not into the running one.
  • Negotiate scope, never quality or sustainable pace.
  • Escalate to the RTE/leadership when pressure is persistent and unreasonable.
✎
Say this "My job isn't to say 'no' — it's to make the cost of 'yes' visible, so stakeholders make an informed call with the PO."
Q6

How would you communicate with stakeholders and regain their confidence after the missed release (Q4)?

  1. Own it — no excuses. Acknowledge the miss and its impact clearly.
  2. Share the root cause and the fix — a concrete, credible corrective plan with dates.
  3. Over-communicate progress — short, regular updates showing the plan is working (burn-up, demos).
  4. Deliver small, visible wins early to rebuild trust incrementally.
  5. Show the systemic change — the metric or process that now prevents a repeat.
★
Principle Confidence is rebuilt with consistency, not one big apology. Predictable delivery over the next few sprints is what actually wins them back.
04 Agile vs Traditional Project Management

Classic PM concepts and how Agile differs

EY blends consulting-style PM with Agile, so they probe both worlds. Know the traditional terms and the Agile contrast.

Q7

How do you manage project risks in traditional project management?

Through a structured risk management process, usually captured in a risk register:

  1. Identify risks (workshops, checklists, expert input).
  2. Analyse — qualitative (probability × impact matrix) and, where needed, quantitative.
  3. Plan responses — the four strategies: Avoid, Mitigate, Transfer, Accept (plus Exploit/Enhance for opportunities).
  4. Add contingency — schedule and budget reserves.
  5. Monitor & control — review regularly, track triggers, update the register.
★
Bridge to Agile "In Agile/SAFe I use the same thinking but lighter — a RAID log at team level and ROAM (Resolved, Owned, Accepted, Mitigated) for program risks at PI Planning."
Q8

What is the difference between Agile and Traditional Project Management?

The "iron triangle" flips: Waterfall fixes scope; Agile fixes time and cost and flexes scope.

✎
One-liner "Traditional PM plans everything up front and delivers once; Agile delivers in small increments, inspects, and adapts. Waterfall suits stable requirements; Agile suits uncertainty and change."
Q25

How do you do budget planning in project management?

Traditional: estimate costs bottom-up from the work breakdown, apply techniques like analogous (based on similar past projects), parametric (rate × units), and bottom-up estimating; aggregate into a cost baseline; add management and contingency reserves; then track actuals against plan.

In SAFe / Lean: we fund value streams, not projects — Lean budgets with guardrails, and participatory budgeting each PI. This avoids the overhead of per-project cost accounting and keeps funding flowing to value.

Q27

What do you understand by "burn rate"?

Burn rate is the speed at which you consume a resource — usually budget — over time. E.g. spending ₹10 L/month is your monthly burn rate. Compared against planned spend and earned value, it tells you whether you'll run out of budget before the work is done.

★
Agile angle Don't confuse it with a burndown chart (work remaining). Burn rate is about money/resource consumption vs plan.
Q28

What do you understand by the "critical path"?

The critical path is the longest chain of dependent tasks through a project — it determines the shortest possible project duration. Tasks on it have zero float/slack: any delay to a critical-path task delays the whole project. It tells you where to focus attention and where a slip actually hurts.

05 Retrospectives

Keeping retros fresh and actionable

Q9

What techniques do you use in Sprint Retrospectives?

I vary the format so retros don't go stale, and always end with 1–2 owned action items.

  • Start / Stop / Continue — simple and fast.
  • Mad / Sad / Glad — surfaces emotions and morale.
  • Sailboat / Speedboat — wind (helps), anchors (holds back), rocks (risks).
  • 4Ls — Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed for.
  • Starfish — more of / less of / keep / start / stop.
  • 5 Whys — for drilling into a specific recurring problem.

I use dot voting to prioritise, keep it psychologically safe, and track previous actions at the top — nothing kills a retro faster than "nothing ever changes."

06 AI for Scrum Masters

How you actually use AI in the role

This was a heavy theme (four questions). Be concrete and balanced — AI augments you, it doesn't replace judgment, and be mindful of data privacy.

Q10

How comfortable are you with AI as a Scrum Master?

"Very comfortable, and I treat it as a productivity multiplier for the administrative and analytical parts of my role — while keeping the human coaching and facilitation firmly with me." Then back it with the specifics below.

Q11 · Q12 · Q13

Have you implemented AI in your team? How do you leverage it day-to-day?

Give concrete, believable use-cases (adjust to what you've genuinely done):

  • Backlog & stories — drafting user stories and acceptance criteria, then refining with the team; checking stories against INVEST.
  • Meeting support — summarising Daily Scrum notes, retros and reviews into action items.
  • Reporting — turning raw sprint data into stakeholder-ready status summaries.
  • Analysis — spotting patterns in defects, spillover, or cycle-time data; drafting risk descriptions for the RAID log.
  • Test support — helping generate test-case outlines from acceptance criteria.
★
Balance to show "I always keep a human in the loop, validate AI output, and I'm careful about not putting sensitive/client data into public tools. AI speeds up my prep so I spend more time coaching people."
Q14b

Are you using AI within Jira?

Yes — Atlassian Intelligence in Jira/Confluence supports: natural-language JQL generation ("show me all blocked stories in this sprint"), summarising long issues and comment threads, drafting descriptions and acceptance criteria, and AI-assisted automation rules. If your org hasn't enabled it, say so honestly and describe how you'd use it.

▲
Don't over-claim If you haven't used a specific feature, say "I've used X, and I'm aware Jira also offers Y" — interviewers probe exaggeration quickly.
07 Cross-Team Dependencies

Coordinating work across the ART

Q14

How do you manage cross-team dependencies?

  • Identify early — at PI Planning, on the program board (the red strings between teams).
  • Visualise & own — every dependency has an owner and a target iteration.
  • Coordinate regularly — Scrum of Scrums and the ART sync, plus a PO sync for scope-level dependencies.
  • De-risk — ROAM them; sequence work so the providing team delivers first; use clear interfaces/API contracts and feature toggles to decouple.
  • Escalate blockers to the RTE when teams can't resolve them alone.
✎
Say this "The goal is to make dependencies visible and owned before they become blockers — the program board and Scrum of Scrums are my main tools."
08 Agile & Scrum Fundamentals

The values — know them cold

Q15

Name the 4 Agile values and explain one with a real-life example from your work.

The four values of the Agile Manifesto — "we value the left more than the right":

  1. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  2. Working software over comprehensive documentation
  3. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  4. Responding to change over following a plan
✎
Example (customise) "'Responding to change over following a plan' — mid-PI a key client priority shifted. Instead of forcing the original plan, we re-ordered the backlog, adjusted the next sprint, and delivered the newly-critical feature first. We flexed scope to protect value."
Q16

Explain the 5 Scrum values.

  • Commitment — the team commits to goals and to supporting each other.
  • Focus — everyone focuses on the Sprint's work and goal.
  • Courage — to tackle hard problems and raise uncomfortable truths.
  • Respect — treating each other as capable professionals.
  • Openness — being transparent about work and challenges.

Memory hook: C-F-C-R-O. "The events give structure; the values make the structure actually work."

09 SAFe Metrics & Tracking

Predictability, features, and flow

Q17

What SAFe metrics do you use? Which are most important for your role?

SAFe groups metrics into Outcomes, Flow, and Competency. The ones I use most:

  • PI Predictability Measure — actual vs planned business value per team (target 80–100%). The headline ART metric.
  • Flow metrics — Flow Velocity, Flow Time, Flow Load (WIP), Flow Efficiency, Flow Distribution.
  • Team velocity & say/do ratio — for predictability at team level.
  • Feature/Enabler progress and cumulative flow.
  • Quality — defect leakage / escaped defects.
★
Most important for an SM "Team + ART predictability and flow — they tell me whether the team is reliable and where work is getting stuck, which is exactly what I coach against."
Q18

How do you track PI Predictability? How do you track Feature progress?

PI Predictability: each team sets PI Objectives with business value assigned by Business Owners at planning. At the end of the PI, Business Owners score actual value delivered. Predictability = actual BV ÷ planned BV (committed objectives only) — a healthy team lands 80–100%. Uncommitted objectives count in the actual but not the planned, so they're upside.

Feature progress: a feature burn-up or % of child stories completed, tracked on the program board / in Jira (or Jira Align). I watch it sprint-by-sprint so a lagging feature is caught mid-PI, not at the end.

10 Jira Mastery

Dashboards, hygiene, and JQL

EY drilled hard into Jira. Be specific and practical; they can smell bluffing.

Q19 · Q20

Have you built Jira dashboards? How good are you — rate yourself out of 10.

Yes — describe the gadgets you use: Sprint Burndown, Velocity Chart, Cumulative Flow Diagram, Control Chart (cycle time), Created vs Resolved, Sprint Health, and filter-based issue lists built on saved JQL.

✎
Rating tip Give an honest, confident number with justification — e.g. "8/10 — I build and maintain team and ART dashboards, write JQL, and configure boards/workflows; I'm not a Jira admin doing deep app/plugin configuration." A calibrated 8 beats a hollow 10.
Q21

How do you maintain Jira hygiene?

  • Every item has an owner, estimate, and correct status; statuses updated daily.
  • No stale items — nothing sitting "In Progress" for days without movement.
  • Stories linked to epics/features; no orphans; dependencies linked.
  • Acceptance criteria present before a story is "Ready"; DoD met before "Done".
  • Backlog regularly refined and re-ordered; done items closed; sprint scope kept honest.
Q22

Give 5 JQL queries you use for hygiene maintenance.

They said "prompts" — they mean JQL queries. Have these ready:

1. Unestimated stories in the current sprint:

project = ABC AND sprint in openSprints() AND issuetype = Story AND "Story Points[Number]" is EMPTY

2. Stale "In Progress" items (no update in 3 days):

project = ABC AND status = "In Progress" AND updated <= -3d

3. Blocked stories:

project = ABC AND status = Blocked OR (issueLinkType = "is blocked by" AND resolution = Unresolved)

4. Stories missing acceptance criteria / not epic-linked (orphans):

project = ABC AND issuetype = Story AND "Epic Link" is EMPTY

5. Unresolved high/critical bugs:

project = ABC AND issuetype = Bug AND priority in (High, Highest) AND resolution = Unresolved ORDER BY created ASC
★
Bonus Mention you save these as filters powering dashboard gadgets, so hygiene is monitored continuously, not manually.
11 Key Concepts

Capability, Value Stream Mapping, and slicing

Q23

Are you handling any "Capability" in your team?

In SAFe, a Capability is a higher-level behaviour of a solution — it lives at the Large Solution level, above a Feature, and often spans multiple ARTs. A Capability is to a Solution Train what a Feature is to an ART: it's sized to fit within a single PI and is split into Features.

✎
Answer honestly If you work at Essential SAFe (single ART), say: "My team works at the Feature level; Capabilities apply at the Large Solution level, which I understand but my current ART doesn't operate at."
Q24

Do you understand Value Stream Mapping?

Yes — Value Stream Mapping visualises every step needed to deliver value from trigger to outcome (concept to cash). For each step you capture process time, lead/wait time, and %Complete-and-Accurate, then compute flow efficiency (value-add time ÷ total time). It exposes bottlenecks, hand-off delays, and waste so you can improve flow.

SAFe distinguishes operational value streams (how the business delivers value to customers) from development value streams (how we build the solutions that enable them).

Q29

What is horizontal and vertical slicing?

Agile prefers vertical slices — each is a thin, shippable piece of real functionality.

  • Horizontal slicing — splitting work by technical layer (do all UI, then all backend, then all DB). No single slice delivers usable value.
  • Vertical slicing — a thin slice through all layers that delivers a small piece of working, testable, valuable functionality. This is what we want for user stories.
12 PI Planning — Deep Dive

The two-day event, end to end

This was the big one. Walk it chronologically and hit every sub-question they listed.

Q30

Explain the two-day PI Planning end to end.

Standard two-day agenda. Teal blocks are the team breakouts — where the Scrum Master is most hands-on.

Before PI Planning (readiness): three kinds of prep — content readiness (PM/PO have the vision and top 10 features prioritised, often via WSJF; Product Backlog refined), planning readiness (scope/context aligned, teams known), and logistics readiness (facility/tooling, program board ready).

Day 1:

  1. Business context — a Business Owner explains the current state and portfolio direction.
  2. Product/Solution vision — Product Management presents the vision and top features.
  3. Architecture vision & dev practices — System Architect + a planning-process overview from the RTE.
  4. Team breakouts #1 — teams estimate capacity per iteration, pull features, write draft stories, identify dependencies and risks, and draft their PI objectives.
  5. Draft plan review — each team presents; Management review & problem-solving — leadership adjusts scope/priorities/resourcing to resolve conflicts.

Day 2:

  1. Planning adjustments — RTE/management share overnight changes.
  2. Team breakouts #2 — teams finalise plans, objectives, and dependencies.
  3. Final plan review — each team presents its final plan, objectives (with business value), risks.
  4. ART risks — ROAM — program risks are Resolved / Owned / Accepted / Mitigated openly.
  5. Confidence vote, then plan rework if confidence is low, then a brief planning retrospective and next steps.

Role of the Scrum Master: facilitate the team breakouts, manage capacity, help draft and refine PI objectives, surface and track dependencies/risks, coordinate with other SMs and the RTE, keep the team on time, and drive the confidence vote for the team.

Breakout sessions: the core working time — teams build a sprint-by-sprint plan on the board, write stories, size to capacity, and post dependencies.

How dependencies are identified: on the program board — features per team per iteration, with strings/links connecting a team's item to the team it depends on, so the whole ART sees them.

Confidence vote: a fist-of-five vote on the team's/ART's confidence in meeting the PI objectives. Average 3+ proceeds; below 3, the team re-plans. Anyone voting 1–2 must be heard.

★
Committed vs uncommitted objectives Committed objectives are ones the team is highly confident of and they count toward the PI Predictability measure. Uncommitted (stretch) objectives are lower-certainty — they're planned and give visibility and a buffer, but are not counted in the commitment (they count in actuals if delivered). They protect predictability while keeping stretch work visible.
13 Closing

The environment check and your questions

Q26

"Can you show me your current table / room?" (camera pan)

This is a logistics/authenticity check (common in remote interviews to confirm a professional, private setup and no unfair aids). Handle it calmly and comply — pan the camera, keep it light. A tidy, quiet, distraction-free background is all they're looking for.

★
Prep Before any interview: clean, neutral background, good lighting, no notes visible on screen or desk, phone away. It signals professionalism.
Q31

Do you have any questions for me?

Always have 2–3 ready — it signals genuine interest. Strong options:

  • "How mature is the SAFe adoption here — are you at Essential, Large Solution, or Portfolio level?"
  • "What does success look like for this Scrum Master role in the first 6 months?"
  • "How many teams are on the ART, and what are the biggest delivery challenges right now?"
  • "How does the organisation currently measure predictability and flow?"
  • "What's the biggest impediment you'd want this person to tackle first?"
▲
Avoid "I have no questions" (reads as disinterest) and opening with salary/leave. Ask about the team, the role, and the challenges first.

Interview mindset for EY.
This round blends SAFe depth, traditional PM literacy, hands-on Jira, and practical AI use. For scenario questions, narrate your thinking: acknowledge → get curious about root cause → be transparent early → coach/facilitate rather than command → put in a proactive fix. For factual ones, be crisp and use the real terms.
