01 Your Positioning

Who you are and how you frame your role

Opening, identity-setting questions. Answer honestly, but frame yourself as someone already operating beyond just running ceremonies.

Q1

Are you managing at a team level, or are you a designated Agile Coach?

Over my six years I've grown from running ceremonies for a single team into a coaching role, so I position myself in the middle and show the range of what I do.

◆ In my experience"My designated title is Senior Scrum Master, and I currently support two Scrum teams on a five-team ART delivering our healthcare booking and telehealth platform. Alongside the day-to-day facilitation, I run our quarterly Agile maturity assessments, mentor a newer Scrum Master on the train, and coach a first-time Product Owner on backlog health. So officially I'm a team-level SM, but in practice I already operate with a coaching footprint across the ART."

The distinction I make sure to show I understand: a Scrum Master serves one or two teams and the process; an Agile Coach works across teams, leadership and the wider organisation on the transformation and mindset.

02 Agile Maturity & Metrics

Assessing how mature a team really is

They want to hear that maturity is multi-dimensional and trend-based — never a single vanity number.

Q2

How do you assess the Agile maturity of a team? What metrics do you use, and how do you evaluate?

I assess maturity across several dimensions, not one score, combining a structured self-assessment with objective delivery data over a few sprints.

The dimensions I look at:

  • Delivery predictability — does the team reliably meet its Sprint Goal and PI objectives?
  • Quality & engineering practices — CI/CD, test automation, low escaped defects, controlled tech debt.
  • Self-organisation — does the team plan and solve problems without me driving every decision?
  • Collaboration & transparency — a healthy backlog, honest Daily Scrums, retros with real follow-through.
  • Value focus — does the team understand the "why" and the patient/clinic outcome, not just output?

Metrics I use: say/do ratio (commitment reliability), velocity stability (not size), sprint and PI predictability, defect leakage, cycle time and flow efficiency, and team happiness. I overlay a SAFe / Agility Health–style radar the team fills in themselves, so the picture is theirs, not mine.

◆ In my experience"When I joined my current ART, one team looked productive — velocity around 50 — but had roughly 30% escaped defects and spilled work every sprint. My assessment across delivery, quality and self-organisation showed the team was actually immature despite the velocity. Over two PIs, by tightening the Definition of Done and refining smaller, we lifted say/do from ~68% to ~90% and cut escaped defects by more than half."
★
Evaluation "I read trends over three to four sprints, never a snapshot, and I use it to start a coaching conversation — not to grade the team. A high velocity with poor quality is low maturity."
03 Delivery Challenges & Scenarios

Spillover, missed releases, and stakeholder pressure

Scenario questions test judgment. The winning pattern: get curious about root cause, be transparent early, protect quality, and put in proactive prevention.

Q3

How do you handle recurring spillover of user stories? What have you done to improve it?

I treat recurring spillover as a symptom — a retrospective topic — and chase the root cause rather than pushing the team harder.

The common root causes and how I address each:

  • Over-commitment → plan to capacity and historical velocity, not optimism; leave a buffer for production support.
  • Stories too big → stronger refinement and vertical slicing so items finish inside one sprint.
  • Weak Definition of Ready → don't pull a story in until it's clear, estimated and dependency-free.
  • Mid-sprint scope / interruptions → protect the Sprint Goal and make unplanned work visible.
  • Hidden dependencies → surface them in planning and the Scrum of Scrums.
◆ In my experience"On my team, our appointment-booking stories kept spilling because they were written end-to-end — 'patient books an appointment' as a single 13-pointer that touched UI, the scheduling service and the clinic PMS integration. We started vertically slicing them (first a single provider, fixed slot; then multi-provider; then real-time availability) and enforced a Definition of Ready. Spillover dropped from around a third of the sprint to under 10%, and our say/do ratio settled above 85%."
Q4

A critical feature misses a SAFe release. How do you handle it — immediate actions and proactive measures?

Immediate actions — transparency first:

  1. Raise it early, the moment the risk is real — not at the deadline. I inform the PO, the RTE and stakeholders.
  2. Assess impact honestly — what's done, what isn't, and the options.
  3. Explore alternatives: descope to an MVP, ship behind a feature toggle, or release a thin slice that still gives value.
  4. Coordinate at ART level through the RTE for cross-team dependencies; re-plan the remainder into the next iteration or PI.
  5. Run a quick root-cause review so the miss becomes a learning, not a blame exercise.
◆ In my experience"We were due to release telehealth video consultations for a PI milestone, and a third-party video SDK integration slipped. Rather than miss the whole thing, we flagged it a week out, shipped the booking and waiting-room flow behind a feature flag, and released the live video two weeks later once it was stable. Stakeholders got value on time and we protected quality — no half-tested video going to patients."

Proactive measures to prevent a repeat:

  • Define MVP vs nice-to-have up front, so there's always a shippable core.
  • Realistic PI planning with capacity buffers and uncommitted (stretch) objectives.
  • Actively manage dependencies and ROAM program risks at planning.
  • Continuous integration and incremental delivery — integrate every sprint, not at the end.
  • Feature flags so incomplete work can ship dark and be switched on when ready.
Q5

How would you protect your team from stakeholder pressure?

  • Single channel: all work flows through the Product Owner and the backlog — no side-assignments to developers.
  • Use data, not opinions: I show capacity and velocity so "can we also add this?" becomes a visible trade-off.
  • Protect the Sprint Goal: new asks go to the backlog for the next sprint, not into the running one.
  • Negotiate scope — never quality or sustainable pace.
  • Escalate to the RTE / leadership when the pressure is persistent and unreasonable.
◆ In my experience"A clinic-facing stakeholder kept dropping 'urgent' tweaks straight onto a developer mid-sprint. I set up a simple rule — everything comes to the PO — and started showing the sprint's committed capacity in our review. Once the stakeholder could see that each new ask pushed out a patient-facing feature they cared about, the interruptions basically stopped and the requests went into refinement instead."
✎
Say this "My job isn't to say 'no' — it's to make the cost of 'yes' visible so the stakeholder and PO make an informed call together."
Q6

How would you communicate with stakeholders and regain their confidence after the missed release (Q4)?

  1. Own it — no excuses. Acknowledge the miss and its impact clearly.
  2. Share the root cause and the fix — a concrete, credible corrective plan with dates.
  3. Over-communicate progress — short, regular updates showing the plan is working (a burn-up, a demo).
  4. Deliver small, visible wins early to rebuild trust incrementally.
  5. Show the systemic change — the metric or process that now prevents a repeat.
◆ In my experience"After the telehealth slip, I moved our stakeholders from a monthly update to a short weekly note plus a working demo, and we shipped the video feature in two visible increments. Two sprints of predictable delivery did more for their confidence than any apology — and we kept the weekly demo because they valued the visibility."
★
Principle Confidence comes back through consistency, not one big apology. Predictable delivery over the next few sprints is what actually wins people back.
04 Agile vs Traditional Project Management

Classic PM concepts and how Agile differs

EY blends consulting-style PM with Agile, so they probe both worlds. Know the traditional terms and the Agile contrast.

Q7

How do you manage project risks in traditional project management?

Through a structured risk management process, usually captured in a risk register:

  1. Identify risks (workshops, checklists, expert input).
  2. Analyse — qualitative (probability × impact matrix) and, where needed, quantitative.
  3. Plan responses — the four strategies: Avoid, Mitigate, Transfer, Accept (plus Exploit/Enhance for opportunities).
  4. Add contingency — schedule and budget reserves.
  5. Monitor & control — review regularly, track triggers, keep the register current.
★
Bridge to Agile "In my SAFe work I use the same thinking but lighter — a RAID log at team level and ROAM (Resolved, Owned, Accepted, Mitigated) for program risks at PI Planning. In healthcare I always carry a compliance/patient-data-privacy risk line explicitly."
Q8

What is the difference between Agile and Traditional Project Management?

The "iron triangle" flips: Waterfall fixes scope; Agile fixes time and cost and flexes scope.

✎
One-liner "Traditional PM plans everything up front and delivers once; Agile delivers in small increments, inspects, and adapts. Waterfall suits stable requirements; Agile suits uncertainty and change — which is most of what we see in patient-facing software."
Q25

How do you do budget planning in project management?

Traditional: estimate costs bottom-up from the work breakdown, apply techniques like analogous (based on similar past projects), parametric (rate × units) and bottom-up estimating; aggregate into a cost baseline; add management and contingency reserves; then track actuals against plan.

In SAFe / Lean: we fund value streams, not projects — Lean budgets with guardrails, and participatory budgeting each PI. This keeps funding flowing to value instead of to fixed per-project cost accounting.

★
Honest framing "As a Scrum Master I don't own the budget, but I contribute the inputs — capacity, velocity and forecast — that the RTE and Business Owners use for Lean budgeting and release forecasting."
Q27

What do you understand by "burn rate"?

Burn rate is the speed at which you consume a resource — usually budget — over time. If a program spends, say, ₹40 lakh a month, that's the monthly burn rate. Compared against planned spend and earned value, it tells you whether you'll run out of budget before the work is done.

★
Don't confuse it with a burndown chart (work remaining). Burn rate is about money / resource consumption against plan.
Q28

What do you understand by the "critical path"?

The critical path is the longest chain of dependent tasks through a project — it sets the shortest possible duration. Tasks on it have zero float: any delay to a critical-path task delays the whole project. It tells you where a slip actually hurts and where to focus attention.

◆ In my experience"For our telehealth launch, the critical path ran through the third-party video integration and its security/compliance sign-off — everything else had slack, but a slip there moved the whole go-live. Knowing that, I made sure that item was de-risked first."
05 Retrospectives

Keeping retros fresh and actionable

Q9

What techniques do you use in Sprint Retrospectives?

I vary the format so retros don't go stale, and I always end with one or two owned action items.

  • Start / Stop / Continue — simple and fast.
  • Mad / Sad / Glad — surfaces morale and emotion.
  • Sailboat — wind (helps), anchors (holds back), rocks (risks).
  • 4Ls — Liked, Learned, Lacked, Longed for.
  • Starfish — more of / less of / keep / start / stop.
  • 5 Whys — to drill into one recurring problem.

I use dot voting to prioritise, keep it psychologically safe, and I review the previous retro's actions first — nothing kills a retro faster than "nothing ever changes."

◆ In my experience"When my team's retros went flat, I switched to the Sailboat and, crucially, started every retro by ticking off last sprint's action items. Once people saw their input actually change things — we automated a painful manual regression check that came out of one retro — engagement came straight back."
06 AI for Scrum Masters

How you actually use AI in the role

This was a heavy theme (four questions). Be concrete and balanced — AI augments you, it doesn't replace judgment, and in healthcare, data privacy is non-negotiable.

Q10

How comfortable are you with AI as a Scrum Master?

"Very comfortable — I treat it as a productivity multiplier for the administrative and analytical parts of my role, while keeping the human coaching and facilitation firmly with me." Then I back it with the specifics below.

Q11 · Q12 · Q13

Have you implemented AI in your team? How do you leverage it day-to-day?

I give concrete, believable use-cases:

  • Backlog & stories — drafting user stories and acceptance criteria, then refining with the team; checking stories against INVEST.
  • Meeting support — summarising retros and reviews into action items.
  • Reporting — turning raw sprint data into stakeholder-ready status summaries.
  • Analysis — spotting patterns in defects, spillover or cycle-time data; drafting risk descriptions for the RAID log.
  • Test support — generating test-case outlines from acceptance criteria.
◆ In my experience"I use AI to turn a messy set of acceptance criteria into a first-draft test-case checklist for our QA, and to summarise a 40-minute retro into clear action items. It saves me a couple of hours a week that I put back into coaching. In healthcare I'm strict about this: no patient data or PHI ever goes into a public AI tool — only sanitised, non-sensitive text."
★
Balance to show "There's always a human in the loop — I validate every output. AI speeds up my prep; it doesn't make the team's decisions."
Q14b

Are you using AI within Jira?

Yes — Atlassian Intelligence in Jira/Confluence supports natural-language JQL generation ("show me all blocked stories in this sprint"), summarising long issues and comment threads, drafting descriptions and acceptance criteria, and AI-assisted automation rules.

▲
Don't over-claim If you haven't used a specific feature, say "I've used X, and I'm aware Jira also offers Y." Interviewers probe exaggeration quickly — a calibrated answer beats a hollow one.
07 Cross-Team Dependencies

Coordinating work across the ART

Q14

How do you manage cross-team dependencies?

  • Identify early — at PI Planning, on the program board (the red strings between teams).
  • Visualise & own — every dependency has an owner and a target iteration.
  • Coordinate regularly — the Scrum of Scrums and ART sync, plus a PO sync for scope-level dependencies.
  • De-risk — ROAM them; sequence work so the providing team delivers first; use clear API contracts and feature toggles to decouple.
  • Escalate blockers to the RTE when teams can't resolve them alone.
◆ In my experience"My team's booking feature depended on the integrations team exposing a real-time slot-availability API from the clinic PMS. At PI Planning we made it an explicit dependency on the program board, agreed the API contract up front, and sequenced their work one iteration ahead of ours. I built a mock to the agreed contract so we weren't blocked while they finished — we integrated for real in the next sprint with almost no rework."
✎
Say this "The goal is to make dependencies visible and owned before they become blockers — the program board and Scrum of Scrums are my main tools."
08 Agile & Scrum Fundamentals

The values — know them cold

Q15

Name the 4 Agile values and explain one with a real-life example from your work.

The four values of the Agile Manifesto — "we value the left more than the right":

  1. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  2. Working software over comprehensive documentation
  3. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  4. Responding to change over following a plan
◆ In my experience"'Responding to change over following a plan' — mid-PI, a regulatory update meant patient-consent capture had to change before we could ship telehealth. Instead of clinging to the original plan, we re-ordered the backlog, brought the consent story forward, and adjusted the next sprint. We flexed scope to protect a compliant, valuable release rather than shipping on the old plan."
Q16

Explain the 5 Scrum values.

  • Commitment — the team commits to goals and to supporting each other.
  • Focus — everyone focuses on the Sprint's work and goal.
  • Courage — to tackle hard problems and raise uncomfortable truths.
  • Respect — treating each other as capable professionals.
  • Openness — being transparent about work and challenges.

Memory hook: C-F-C-R-O. "The events give the structure; the values make the structure actually work."

09 SAFe Metrics & Tracking

Predictability, features, and flow

Q17

What SAFe metrics do you use? Which are most important for your role?

SAFe groups metrics into Outcomes, Flow and Competency. The ones I use most:

  • PI Predictability Measure — actual vs planned business value per team (target 80–100%). The headline ART metric.
  • Flow metrics — Flow Velocity, Flow Time, Flow Load (WIP), Flow Efficiency, Flow Distribution.
  • Team velocity & say/do ratio — for team-level predictability.
  • Feature / Enabler progress and cumulative flow.
  • Quality — defect leakage / escaped defects (which I watch closely in a patient-facing product).
★
Most important for an SM "Team and ART predictability plus flow — they tell me whether the team is reliable and where work is getting stuck, which is exactly what I coach against."
Q18

How do you track PI Predictability? How do you track Feature progress?

PI Predictability: each team sets PI Objectives with business value assigned by Business Owners at planning. At the end of the PI, Business Owners score the actual value delivered. Predictability = actual BV ÷ planned BV (committed objectives only) — a healthy team lands 80–100%. Uncommitted objectives count in the actual but not the planned, so they're upside.

Feature progress: a feature burn-up or the % of child stories completed, tracked on the program board and in Jira / Jira Align. I watch it sprint by sprint so a lagging feature is caught mid-PI, not at the end.

◆ In my experience"My teams run at roughly 90–95% PI predictability. When a feature's burn-up flattened two sprints in, we caught it early, found a hidden dependency on the integrations team, and re-planned — instead of discovering the slip at the system demo."
10 Jira Mastery

Dashboards, hygiene, and JQL

EY drilled hard into Jira. Be specific and practical — they can smell bluffing.

Q19 · Q20

Have you built Jira dashboards? How good are you — rate yourself out of 10.

Yes — I build and maintain team and ART dashboards. The gadgets I rely on: Sprint Burndown, Velocity Chart, Cumulative Flow Diagram, Control Chart (cycle time), Created vs Resolved, Sprint Health, and filter-based issue lists built on saved JQL.

✎
Rating "I'd say a confident 8/10 — I build dashboards, write JQL, and configure boards and workflows day to day. I'm not a Jira administrator doing deep app/plugin or permission-scheme configuration, so I won't claim a 10." A calibrated 8 with a reason beats a hollow 10.
Q21

How do you maintain Jira hygiene?

  • Every item has an owner, estimate and correct status; statuses updated daily.
  • No stale items — nothing sitting "In Progress" for days without movement.
  • Stories linked to epics / features; no orphans; dependencies linked.
  • Acceptance criteria present before a story is "Ready"; DoD met before "Done".
  • Backlog regularly refined and re-ordered; done items closed; sprint scope kept honest.
◆ In my experience"I run a five-minute 'board hygiene' check before every Daily and a slightly deeper one before refinement, driven by saved filters on a dashboard — so hygiene is monitored continuously rather than cleaned up in a panic before PI Planning."
Q22

Give 5 JQL queries you use for hygiene maintenance.

They said "prompts" — they mean JQL queries. I keep these saved as filters:

1. Unestimated stories in the current sprint:

project = HEALTH AND sprint in openSprints() AND issuetype = Story AND "Story Points[Number]" is EMPTY

2. Stale "In Progress" items (no update in 3 days):

project = HEALTH AND status = "In Progress" AND updated <= -3d

3. Blocked stories:

project = HEALTH AND (status = Blocked OR issueLinkType = "is blocked by") AND resolution = Unresolved

4. Orphan stories (not linked to an epic):

project = HEALTH AND issuetype = Story AND "Epic Link" is EMPTY

5. Unresolved high/critical bugs, oldest first:

project = HEALTH AND issuetype = Bug AND priority in (High, Highest) AND resolution = Unresolved ORDER BY created ASC
★
Bonus "I save each of these as a filter powering a dashboard gadget, so the whole team sees hygiene issues live."
11 Key Concepts

Capability, Value Stream Mapping, and slicing

Q23

Are you handling any "Capability" in your team?

In SAFe, a Capability is a higher-level behaviour of a solution — it lives at the Large Solution level, above a Feature, and often spans multiple ARTs. A Capability is to a Solution Train what a Feature is to an ART: it's sized to fit within a PI and is split into Features.

✎
Answer honestly "My ART works at the Feature level — Essential SAFe — so I work with Features, not Capabilities. I understand Capabilities apply at the Large Solution level, but my current train doesn't operate there."
Q24

Do you understand Value Stream Mapping?

Yes — Value Stream Mapping visualises every step needed to deliver value from trigger to outcome (concept to cash). For each step you capture process time, lead/wait time and %Complete-and-Accurate, then compute flow efficiency (value-add time ÷ total time). It exposes bottlenecks, hand-off delays and waste.

SAFe distinguishes operational value streams (how the business delivers value — e.g. a patient booking and attending an appointment) from development value streams (how we build the solutions that enable them).

◆ In my experience"We mapped our release value stream and found the biggest delay wasn't coding — it was a multi-day wait for a manual compliance/security review before each release. Making that wait visible let us start the review earlier and in parallel, cutting our lead time noticeably."
Q29

What is horizontal and vertical slicing?

Agile prefers vertical slices — each is a thin, shippable piece of real functionality.

  • Horizontal slicing — splitting by technical layer (all UI, then all backend, then all DB). No single slice delivers usable value.
  • Vertical slicing — a thin slice through all layers that delivers a small piece of working, testable, valuable functionality. This is what we want for user stories.
◆ In my experience"For appointment booking we sliced vertically: slice 1 was 'book a fixed 30-minute slot with one provider' — real UI, real API, real DB, shippable. Then 'choose from multiple providers', then 'real-time availability'. Each slice was demoable and gave the clinic something usable, instead of a half-built UI with no working booking."
12 PI Planning — Deep Dive

The two-day event, end to end

This was the big one. Walk it chronologically and hit every sub-question they listed.

Q30

Explain the two-day PI Planning end to end.

Standard two-day agenda. Teal blocks are the team breakouts — where the Scrum Master is most hands-on.

Before PI Planning (readiness): three kinds of prep — content readiness (PM/PO have the vision and top features prioritised, often via WSJF; backlog refined), planning readiness (scope and context aligned, teams known) and logistics readiness (facility/tooling, program board ready).

Day 1:

  1. Business context — a Business Owner explains the current state and market/portfolio direction.
  2. Product / Solution vision — Product Management presents the vision and top features.
  3. Architecture vision & dev practices — the System Architect, plus a planning-process overview from the RTE.
  4. Team breakouts #1 — teams estimate capacity per iteration, pull features, draft stories, identify dependencies and risks, and draft their PI objectives.
  5. Draft plan review, then Management review & problem-solving — leadership adjusts scope, priorities and resourcing to resolve conflicts.

Day 2:

  1. Planning adjustments — the RTE/management share overnight changes.
  2. Team breakouts #2 — teams finalise plans, objectives and dependencies.
  3. Final plan review — each team presents its final plan, objectives (with business value) and risks.
  4. ART risks — ROAM — program risks are Resolved / Owned / Accepted / Mitigated openly.
  5. Confidence vote, plan rework if confidence is low, then a brief planning retrospective and next steps.

Role of the Scrum Master: I facilitate the team breakouts, manage capacity, help draft and refine PI objectives, surface and track dependencies and risks, coordinate with the other SMs and the RTE, keep the team on time, and run the confidence vote for my team.

Breakout sessions: the core working time — teams build a sprint-by-sprint plan on the board, write stories, size to capacity and post dependencies.

How dependencies are identified: on the program board — features per team per iteration, with strings/links connecting a team's item to the team it depends on, so the whole ART can see them.

Confidence vote: a fist-of-five vote on the team's/ART's confidence in meeting the PI objectives. Average 3 or more proceeds; below 3, the team re-plans. Anyone voting 1–2 must be heard.

★
Committed vs uncommitted objectives Committed objectives are ones the team is highly confident of, and they count toward the PI Predictability measure. Uncommitted (stretch) objectives are lower-certainty — planned and visible, giving a buffer, but not counted in the commitment (they count in actuals if delivered). They protect predictability while keeping stretch work visible.
◆ In my experience"In our last PI, the third-party video integration was a genuine unknown, so we set it as an uncommitted objective and committed to the booking and waiting-room flow. That protected our predictability score, kept the stretch goal visible to stakeholders, and when the integration landed early it counted as upside."
13 Closing

The environment check and your questions

Q26

"Can you show me your current table / room?" (camera pan)

This is a logistics / authenticity check — common in remote interviews to confirm a professional, private setup with no unfair aids. I handle it calmly and comply — pan the camera, keep it light. A tidy, quiet, distraction-free background is all they're looking for.

★
Prep Before any interview: clean, neutral background, good lighting, no notes visible on screen or desk, phone away. It signals professionalism.
Q31

Do you have any questions for me?

Always have two or three ready — it signals genuine interest. Strong options:

  • "How mature is the SAFe adoption here — are you at Essential, Large Solution or Portfolio level?"
  • "What does success look like for this Scrum Master role in the first six months?"
  • "How many teams are on the ART, and what are the biggest delivery challenges right now?"
  • "How does the organisation currently measure predictability and flow?"
  • "What's the biggest impediment you'd want this person to tackle first?"
▲
Avoid "I have no questions" (reads as disinterest) and opening with salary/leave. Ask about the team, the role and the challenges first.
