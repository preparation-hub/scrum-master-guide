02 User Stories & INVEST

Where Zemoso probes hardest

Expect rapid "which one is missing?" follow-ups — know the 3 C's and INVEST cold.

The six qualities of a good user story.

Q2

What are the three C's of a user story?

  • Card — the short written story (a placeholder for a conversation).
  • Conversation — the discussion that fleshes out the detail.
  • Confirmation — the acceptance criteria that confirm it's done.
◆ In my experience"For 'patient reschedules an appointment,' the card is one line; the conversation answered 'how late can they reschedule? what about paid slots?'; and the confirmation was the AC — 'cannot reschedule within 2 hours of the slot.'"
Q3

If developers still have many questions about a Jira story, which of the three C's is missing?

The Conversation. Lots of open questions means the discussion that turns a placeholder card into shared understanding hasn't happened — usually a sign refinement was skipped.

Q4

What are the characteristics of a good user story?

They follow INVEST: Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable (see diagram). In short: a thin, independently valuable slice with clear acceptance criteria that fits comfortably in a Sprint.

Q5

If a story is valuable, estimable and testable but can't be completed without another story, which INVEST principle is missing?

Independent. Needing another story to be completable means it isn't independent — a dependency that should be resolved (or the two stories merged/re-sliced) before it enters a Sprint.

Q6

Which INVEST principle is most commonly violated?

Usually Independent or Small — real-world stories tend to be too big and tangled with dependencies. That's exactly why vertical slicing matters.

◆ In my experience"Our booking stories kept violating 'Small' and 'Independent' — one 13-point story spanned UI, scheduling and the clinic PMS. Slicing it vertically (one provider → multi-provider → real-time availability) fixed both and killed our spillover."
Q7

A backlog item like "build an online banking system" — is it a user story, feature, or epic?

An Epic — a very large body of work spanning many Sprints (arguably even an initiative). It must be broken into Features, then into small user Stories before it can be built.

Q8

For a good user story, which is more important: a detailed story or a valuable story?

A valuable story. Detail emerges through the conversation just-in-time; value is the reason to build it at all. An exhaustively detailed but low-value story is waste; a valuable one with light detail can be fleshed out as it's pulled in.

Q9

If a user story has detailed acceptance criteria, do we still need conversation?

Yes. Acceptance criteria capture the what, but the conversation surfaces the why, edge cases, and technical trade-offs. AC are an output of good conversation, not a replacement for it.

◆ In my experience"Our reschedule story had crisp AC, but the 5-minute conversation revealed a timezone edge case for cross-clinic bookings that no AC had captured. The talk still adds value even when the card looks complete."
03 Scrum Fundamentals

Pillars, structure, DoD/DoR, accountability

Q10

What are the three pillars of Scrum?

Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation — the pillars that hold up Scrum's empirical process control.

Q11

Could you elaborate on the three pillars?

  • Transparency — the work and progress are visible and honest (shared board, clear DoD).
  • Inspection — you check work and process regularly (that's what the events are for).
  • Adaptation — you adjust quickly when inspection reveals a problem.

The chain: you can only adapt what you inspect, and only inspect what's transparent.

◆ In my experience"When a team hid that a feature was only '80% done,' there was no transparency — so the Review couldn't inspect reality and nobody adapted, and it blew up at release. An honest board + DoD is what makes the other two pillars possible."
Q12

What are the Scrum framework's roles, events, and artifacts?

  • Accountabilities (3): Product Owner, Scrum Master, Developers.
  • Events (5): the Sprint, Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, Sprint Retrospective.
  • Artifacts (3): Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment (each with a commitment: Product Goal, Sprint Goal, Definition of Done).
Q13

What is a Sprint?

A fixed, short cycle (1–4 weeks) in which the team builds a usable increment toward the Sprint Goal. It's the heartbeat of Scrum — a new Sprint starts immediately after the previous one ends, and its length stays consistent.

Q14

What is the exit criteria for DoD and the entry criteria for DoR?

DoR (Definition of Ready) is the entry gate — what a story needs to enter a Sprint (clear, estimated, dependency-free, has AC). DoD (Definition of Done) is the exit gate — what it needs to leave as truly finished (tested, reviewed, deployed, compliant). DoR guards the start; DoD guards the finish.

★
Note DoD is official Scrum; DoR is a widely-used practice but not in the Scrum Guide.
Q15

If Scrum has no project manager, who is responsible if a Sprint fails?

No single person — the Scrum Team owns delivery collectively. Scrum deliberately removes the single-throat-to-choke model. A "failed" Sprint is a learning signal to inspect and adapt in the Retro, not a hunt for who to blame.

Q16

Why does Scrum promote collective ownership instead of individual blame?

Because complex product work is a team outcome, and blame kills the transparency Scrum depends on. Collective ownership builds psychological safety, so people raise problems early instead of hiding them — which is how the team actually improves.

◆ In my experience"After a prod incident I steered the Retro to 'what in our process let this through?' not 'who did it?' The fix was a missing automated check. People saw honesty was safe and started flagging near-misses before they became incidents."
04 Agile Values & the Jira Trap

Manifesto values — and the classic "tools" trap

Zemoso loves the "individuals over tools / Jira" line of questioning. Know the intent, not just the words.

Q17

What are the Agile values?

The four values of the Agile Manifesto — "we value the left more than the right":

  1. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  2. Working software over comprehensive documentation
  3. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  4. Responding to change over following a plan
Q18

Are there four principles or four values?

▲
Watch the wording There are 4 values and 12 principles. This question is checking you don't muddle the two.
Q19

Does "individuals and interactions over processes and tools" mean processes and tools are not important?

No. "Over" means we prioritise the left, not that the right is worthless. Good processes and tools matter — they should serve people and collaboration, not the other way round.

Q20

If Jira is down, will development stop?

No. Jira is a tool that supports the work; it isn't the work. If it's down, the team keeps building and collaborating — tracking on a whiteboard or sticky notes until it's back. Developers writing code don't need Jira to write code.

Q21

If the team can continue without Jira using whiteboards or sticky notes, why do companies spend so much on Jira?

Because at scale, tools like Jira provide what sticky notes can't: a single source of truth across distributed teams, history and audit trails, traceability (story → code → release — vital in regulated healthcare), reporting and metrics, integrations, and search. The value is coordination and transparency at scale, not the day-to-day coding.

◆ In my experience"A co-located team of 5 could run on a physical board. But our five-team ART, partly remote, with compliance audit needs, would be chaos without Jira — we need cross-team dependency links, release traceability and dashboards. Jira earns its cost through scale and governance, not because a whiteboard can't hold tasks."
05 Jira & JQL

Hands-on tool questions

Be practical and specific — have real JQL ready.

Q22

What is a Jira workflow?

The set of statuses and transitions an issue moves through — e.g. To Do → In Progress → In Review → Done — with rules on who can move it and what must happen at each step. It models your team's process inside Jira.

Q23

What is JQL?

Jira Query Language — a SQL-like language for searching and filtering issues precisely (e.g. project = HEALTH AND status = Blocked). It powers advanced search, saved filters and dashboard gadgets.

Q24

What is the difference between basic search and JQL?

Basic search is a point-and-click, drop-down filter UI — easy but limited. JQL (advanced search) is the text query language — far more powerful, supporting complex logic (AND/OR/NOT), functions (openSprints(), currentUser()), and ordering. Basic search is a subset of what JQL can express.

Q25

How do you find all work assigned to a team member using JQL?

project = HEALTH AND assignee = "jane.doe" AND resolution = Unresolved ORDER BY priority DESC

(Use assignee = currentUser() for "my" work.)

Q26

How do you find unresolved critical bugs using JQL?

project = HEALTH AND issuetype = Bug AND priority in (Critical, Highest) AND resolution = Unresolved ORDER BY created ASC
Q27

How do you find blocked stories in Jira using JQL?

project = HEALTH AND issuetype = Story AND (status = Blocked OR issueLinkType = "is blocked by") AND resolution = Unresolved
★
Tip Say you save these as filters powering dashboard gadgets, so hygiene is monitored continuously rather than manually.
06 Flow Metrics & WSJF

WIP, throughput, cycle time, prioritisation

Q28

What do you understand about WSJF?

Weighted Shortest Job First — a prioritisation model: WSJF = Cost of Delay ÷ Job Size, where Cost of Delay = business value + time criticality + risk-reduction/opportunity. Do the highest-WSJF items first to maximise value per unit of effort.

◆ In my experience"Reminders: high value (fewer no-shows), time-critical (flu season), small size → very high WSJF, so it went ahead of a big 'multi-clinic dashboard.' WSJF made 'small + urgent + valuable beats big + nice-to-have' objective rather than political."
Q29

What is throughput?

The number of work items completed per unit of time (e.g. 8 stories/Sprint). A flow metric and an alternative to velocity that doesn't rely on story points.

Q30

What is the difference between WIP and throughput?

WIP = items in progress right now. Throughput = items finished per period. Little's Law links them: Cycle Time = WIP ÷ Throughput — so lower WIP shortens cycle time and smooths throughput.

◆ In my experience"We had 12 items in progress for 6 people — everything half-done, nothing shipping. Capping WIP at 4 meant you had to finish before starting; throughput actually rose because work stopped stalling."
Q31

What do you understand by cycle time?

The time from when the team starts an item to when it's done. Shorter cycle time = faster delivery and feedback. (Lead time is broader — from request to delivery, including the wait before work starts.)

Q32

Can WSJF be used in Scrum?

Yes. WSJF comes from SAFe but nothing stops a Scrum PO using it to order the Product Backlog. Scrum doesn't prescribe a prioritisation technique, so borrowing WSJF (or MoSCoW, or cost-of-delay) is perfectly valid.

07 Influence & Change

The "CEO wants to drop Scrum" line

Q33

If a CEO says to stop following Scrum from tomorrow, how would you convince them in 2–3 minutes?

Don't defend the label — connect to their goals: "Scrum is just how we get predictable delivery, fast feedback and quality. Which of those is it failing to give you?" Often the real problem is bad implementation, not Scrum. I'd offer to fix the actual pain and show the value with data.

◆ In my experience"An exec called Scrum 'pointless meetings' — the real issue was 90-minute Dailies and demo-less Sprints, i.e. bad Scrum. We fixed the implementation and the complaint vanished. I defended the outcomes, never the label."
Q34

If the CEO still refuses, what will you do?

I'd respect the decision — I lead by influence, not authority. I'd help transition responsibly and keep the practices that create value (short feedback loops, transparency, sustainable pace, retrospectives) whatever we end up calling the process. The goal is outcomes, not orthodoxy.

Q35

Can a Scrum Master force Scrum?

No. A Scrum Master has no command authority — they coach, facilitate and influence. Forced Scrum is "zombie Scrum": ceremonies without buy-in. Adoption has to be chosen by the team and organisation to actually work.

08 Scenarios

Judgment under pressure

Q36

A user story is lacking the final design and the design team wants to deliver it in one day — what would you do as Scrum Master?

Don't let the team sit blocked or build blind. I'd: keep the story out of "in progress" until it's Ready; facilitate design and devs to agree the minimum design needed to start (or run a quick functional spike); pull forward other ready work so the team stays productive for that day; and if the one-day design slips, protect the Sprint Goal by re-planning with the PO. The root-cause fix is a Definition of Ready so design lands before the Sprint.

Q37

What will you do if developers are caught in an internal disagreement about which development technique to apply?

Technical decisions belong to the self-managing Developers — not me. I facilitate: get both options and their trade-offs on the board, timebox the debate, and if they can't converge, suggest a small spike or a time-boxed trial to let evidence decide. I'd also loop in the architect if it's a cross-cutting call. My job is a healthy decision process, not picking the winner.

◆ In my experience"Two devs deadlocked on caching approaches. I timeboxed a 2-hour spike to benchmark both against our real load. The data settled it in a way no amount of arguing would have — and they owned the outcome."
Q38

What will you do if your Product Owner is new to Scrum and not interested in Scrum meetings?

Coach, don't nag. Explain the PO's value and what breaks without them (unclear priorities, rework, a rudderless backlog). Make events worth their time — bring sharp, prepared refinement. Often the best teacher is letting one small consequence play out and reviewing it together, so they connect the dots themselves.

◆ In my experience"My first-time PO skipped refinement, so vague stories caused a mid-Sprint mess. Rather than lecture, I walked them through that mess afterward — they immediately saw the link and started prioritising refinement. Buy-in beats pressure."
Q39

Under your resume you mentioned removing impediments — what's the biggest one you removed? (not "a dependency")

Have a real, specific, measurable story ready — situation → what was blocked → what you did → outcome.

◆ In my experience"Our releases stalled for days on a manual compliance/security sign-off nobody owned. I mapped the release value stream, showed leadership the wait time, and negotiated moving the review earlier and running it in parallel with QA. Release lead time dropped from ~5 days to under 1 — and the pattern stopped recurring. That was organisational, not a simple task dependency."
09 Spikes & Concepts

Spikes, MMF/MVP, ST vs RTE, and MAD/SAD/GLAD

Q40

What do you understand about a spike?

A time-boxed research task to reduce uncertainty — you produce knowledge (or a throwaway prototype), not shippable functionality. Used when a story is too unclear or risky to estimate confidently.

Q41

When do you use a spike in Scrum?

When there's enough uncertainty that the team can't estimate or start safely — an unfamiliar technology, an unclear requirement, or a risky integration. The spike buys just enough knowledge to proceed, then is closed.

◆ In my experience"Before committing to telehealth, we ran a 2-day spike comparing two video SDKs on quality, cost and privacy compliance. The output was a recommendation, which let us estimate the real stories with confidence."
Q42

What are the types of spikes?

Broadly technical and functional spikes, plus commonly-named research and refactoring spikes. All are time-boxed to protect the Sprint.

Q43

What is a technical spike and what is a functional spike?

  • Technical spike — explores a technical approach: feasibility, which library, performance, security (e.g. "can this video SDK meet our latency and privacy needs?").
  • Functional spike — explores how users will interact or clarifies requirements, often via a quick prototype (e.g. mocking the telehealth waiting-room screen to test with users).
Q44

What do you understand by a research spike or refactoring spike?

A research spike gathers knowledge to inform a decision (evaluate options, investigate a problem). A refactoring spike is a time-boxed effort to explore or improve the internal structure of existing code — reducing technical debt without changing external behaviour.

Q45

What do you understand about MMF and MVP?

MVP (Minimum Viable Product) — the smallest release that delivers value and lets you learn from real users. MMF (Minimum Marketable Feature) — the smallest feature chunk worth releasing to market. MVP is about validated learning; MMF is about marketable value.

◆ In my experience"Our booking MVP was 'book a fixed slot at one pilot clinic' — enough to learn if patients would book online at all. Later, 'reschedule + reminders + confirmations' together made an MMF polished enough to roll out network-wide."
Q46

What is the difference between ST and RTE?

In SAFe: the RTE (Release Train Engineer) is the servant leader / "Scrum Master" of an Agile Release Train (one ART). The STE (Solution Train Engineer) plays the same role one level up — for a Solution Train that coordinates multiple ARTs building a large solution. In short: RTE : ART :: STE : Solution Train.

★
Clarify if needed "ST" here almost certainly means Solution Train / STE. If they meant something else, it's fine to ask them to confirm — precise terms matter in SAFe.
Q47

What do you understand about MAD, SAD, and GLAD?

It's a retrospective format — usually called Mad · Sad · Glad — where the team groups feelings about the Sprint into three columns:

  • Glad — what went well / made us happy.
  • Sad — what disappointed us or fell short.
  • Mad — what frustrated or angered us.

It surfaces the emotional health of the team, not just facts — great for spotting morale issues early. The team then votes on what to act on.

◆ In my experience"When our data-only retros went flat, Mad-Sad-Glad surfaced real frustration about constant interruptions that the numbers had hidden. That emotional signal led to a WIP-limit and focus-time change that a metrics-only retro would have missed."
