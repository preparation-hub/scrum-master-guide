01 Agile Fundamentals

The mindset behind everything

Interviews almost always open here. Get these crisp — they set the tone for the rest.

Animated

Same destination, very different risk: Agile is delivering (and de-risking) value the whole way, not betting everything on one big-bang release.

Q1

What is Agile?

Agile is a mindset and a way of working built on delivering value in small, frequent increments, collaborating closely with customers, and adapting to change — rather than rigidly following a big up-front plan. It emerged in 2001 when 17 software leaders, frustrated that huge "plan-everything-first" projects kept failing, wrote the Agile Manifesto. Crucially, Agile isn't a process you install; it's a philosophy that frameworks like Scrum and Kanban put into practice.

Everyday analogy Like cooking while tasting as you go and adjusting the seasoning — rather than following a fixed recipe blindly and only discovering at the very end that the whole dish is too salty.
Worked exampleA hospital wants a patient-booking app. The waterfall way: spend six months writing a full specification, a year building it, and only at launch discover clinics actually wanted SMS appointment reminders far more than the fancy calendar you polished. The Agile way: ship online booking for one clinic in three weeks, watch how real patients use it, and let what you learn reshape what you build next.
Q2

What is the Agile Manifesto?

A short document written in February 2001 by 17 software practitioners at Snowbird, Utah, who were fed up with heavyweight, documentation-driven processes. It captures the philosophy of Agile in 4 values and 12 principles, and it's the foundation every Agile framework (Scrum, Kanban, XP) builds on. It's deliberately a statement of preferences and values, not a rulebook or methodology.

Worth knowingIt grew out of existing lightweight methods (Scrum, XP, Crystal, DSDM). So when an interviewer asks "is Scrum the same as Agile?", you can say: the Manifesto is the shared philosophy; Scrum is one framework that predates and now implements it.
Q3

What are the 4 values of the Agile Manifesto?

"We value the items on the left more than the items on the right":

  1. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  2. Working software over comprehensive documentation
  3. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  4. Responding to change over following a plan
Worked example"Working software over comprehensive documentation" doesn't mean "no docs." On a healthcare project you still need compliance and API documentation — but you'd rather have a working, demoable booking flow in the Sprint Review than a beautiful 80-page design document and nothing running.
▲
Trap "Over" doesn't mean the right side is worthless — documentation and plans still matter, we just value the left more.
Q4

Can you name some of the 12 Agile principles?

You don't need all 12 verbatim — know the themes and a few exactly:

  • Satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.
  • Welcome changing requirements, even late in development.
  • Deliver working software frequently (weeks, not months).
  • Business and developers work together daily.
  • Build projects around motivated individuals; trust them.
  • Working software is the primary measure of progress.
  • Maintain a sustainable pace.
  • Teams reflect and adjust at regular intervals.
Worked exampleThe last one — "reflect and adjust at regular intervals" — is literally why the Retrospective exists. If an interviewer asks "which principle does the Retro come from?", that's it. Connecting a principle to a concrete Scrum practice is exactly what impresses.
Q5

Agile vs Waterfall — what's the difference?

Waterfall is sequential: plan → design → build → test → release, all once, with value arriving only at the very end. Agile does a little of everything in each short iteration and releases usable value repeatedly (see the chart above). Waterfall fixes scope and lets time/cost vary; Agile fixes time and cost and flexes scope. Waterfall suits stable, well-understood requirements (e.g. a regulatory report with fixed rules); Agile suits uncertainty and change (most product work).

Worked exampleOur booking app in Waterfall: the business sees nothing usable until month 18, and any market change in between is expensive to absorb. In Agile: clinics are booking real patients from week 3, and when priorities shift (say, telehealth suddenly matters more), we simply re-order the backlog next Sprint.
★
Balanced answer Don't bash Waterfall — say it's the right tool when requirements are truly fixed. Maturity impresses more than dogma.
Q6

What are the benefits of Agile?

  • Faster value — usable increments early and often, not one big-bang launch.
  • Flexibility — change is welcomed, not penalised.
  • Better quality — continuous testing and feedback build quality in.
  • Transparency — progress is visible every Sprint.
  • Higher engagement — empowered, self-managing teams.
  • Reduced risk — problems surface early, when they're cheap to fix.
Worked exampleThe "reduced risk" point wins interviews. In Waterfall, a wrong assumption made in month 1 can stay hidden until testing in month 15 — enormously expensive. In Agile, that same assumption is exposed at the next Sprint Review, so the blast radius is one Sprint, not the whole project.
Q7

What is iterative and incremental development?

Incremental = building the product in slices, adding a bit more usable functionality each time. Iterative = revisiting and refining what you already built, based on feedback. Agile does both: each Sprint adds an increment and improves earlier work.

Everyday analogy Painting a portrait: incremental = adding the eyes, then the nose, then the mouth; iterative = going back to refine the eyes after seeing how the whole face looks.
Worked example — the classicBuilding a car for a customer who needs transport: the wrong way delivers a wheel, then a chassis, then a body (nothing usable until the end). The iterative-incremental way delivers a skateboard → scooter → bicycle → motorbike → car — the customer can travel (get value and give feedback) at every step, and you learn what they actually need along the way.
Q8

What are the common Agile frameworks?

  • Scrum — the most popular; fixed-length sprints, defined roles and events. Best for feature delivery.
  • Kanban — continuous flow with WIP limits. Best for support/ops/unpredictable work.
  • Extreme Programming (XP) — engineering practices (TDD, pair programming, CI).
  • Scrumban — Scrum structure + Kanban flow; great for maintenance teams.
  • SAFe, LeSS, Nexus — frameworks for scaling Agile across many teams.
Worked exampleA product team building new features → Scrum. A production-support team firefighting incidents → Kanban (you can't "sprint-commit" to unpredictable incidents). Many real orgs run both side by side, which is a great point to make.
Q9

What is empirical process control?

Making decisions based on what you actually observe (evidence and experience) rather than up-front prediction. It's the theory Scrum is built on, resting on three pillars: Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation. It suits complex work where you can't reliably plan everything in advance — the opposite of a "defined" process like a factory assembly line.

Worked exampleYou predicted a feature would take 3 Sprints. After Sprint 1's Review, real usage data shows users don't want half of it. Empiricism says: don't stubbornly follow the plan — inspect the evidence and adapt the backlog. Scrum's events exist precisely to create these inspect-and-adapt moments.
Q10

What does "inspect and adapt" mean?

Regularly examine your product and your process (inspect), and adjust based on what you learn (adapt). Every Scrum event is an inspect-and-adapt loop: the Daily inspects progress to the goal, the Review inspects the product with stakeholders, the Retrospective inspects the way of working.

Worked exampleRetro shows the team keeps getting blocked waiting on a test environment (inspect). They adapt by booking a dedicated environment and adding a check to the Definition of Ready. Next Sprint they inspect again — did it help? That closing of the loop is the whole point; inspection without adaptation is just a meeting.
Q11

What does "fail fast" mean in Agile?

Run small experiments and surface problems early and cheaply, instead of discovering a big failure at the end. Short iterations mean a wrong assumption costs one Sprint, not the whole project. "Fail fast" is really about learning fast — it's not an excuse for sloppiness.

Worked exampleUnsure whether patients will use video consultations? Don't build the full telehealth suite. Ship a tiny pilot to one clinic behind a feature flag, measure uptake for two weeks, and let the data decide whether to invest further. If it flops, you've lost a Sprint — not a year.
Q12

What is a self-organizing (self-managing) team?

A team that decides internally how to do its work — who does what, and how — without being told by a manager. The 2020 Scrum Guide uses the term self-managing (it also expanded this to include managing what they work on within the Sprint). The Scrum Master coaches the team toward this maturity; they don't direct it.

Worked exampleA blocker appears mid-Sprint. In a command-and-control team, everyone waits for the lead to assign a fix. In a self-managing team, a developer simply picks it up, pairs with a tester, and the team re-plans the day in the Daily — no permission needed. The Scrum Master's job is to build that reflex, then step back.
02 Scrum Framework

What Scrum is and how it holds together

The core definition, pillars, values, and the Sprint heartbeat.

Animated

The Scrum cycle — the marker orbits once per Sprint, the heartbeat that never stops.

Q13

What is Scrum?

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps teams deliver value in short cycles called Sprints, by working together, inspecting results frequently, and adapting. It's the most widely used way to do Agile, defined in the ~13-page Scrum Guide by Ken Schwaber and Jeff Sutherland. It deliberately gives you just enough structure — three accountabilities, five events, three artifacts — and no more.

Worked exampleOur healthcare team turns "let patients rebook a cancelled appointment" into a Product Backlog item, pulls it into a two-week Sprint with a clear Sprint Goal, builds and tests it, demos it to clinic staff at the Review, and improves how they work in the Retro. That full loop — plan, build, inspect, adapt — is Scrum in action.
Q14

Why is Scrum a "framework" and not a "methodology"?

Because it's deliberately incomplete — it defines the minimum rules (roles, events, artifacts) and leaves the rest to the team. A methodology prescribes step-by-step how; a framework is a container you fill with your own practices.

Worked exampleScrum tells you to hold Sprint Planning and produce a Sprint Backlog — but it never says how to estimate (points? t-shirt sizes?), how to test, or which tools to use. One healthcare team uses Planning Poker and TDD; another uses no-estimates and pair testing. Both are doing Scrum correctly. That freedom is why it's a framework.
Q15

What are the three pillars of Scrum?

  • Transparency — work and progress are visible and honest to everyone.
  • Inspection — you check the work and the process regularly.
  • Adaptation — you adjust quickly when inspection shows something's off.

They hold up empiricism: you can only adapt what you inspect, and only inspect what's transparent.

Worked exampleIf a team hides that a booking feature is only "80% done" (no transparency), the Review can't inspect reality, so no one adapts — and the gap explodes at release. A visible board + an honest Definition of Done is what makes the other two pillars even possible.
Q16

What are the five Scrum values?

Commitment, Focus, Courage, Respect, Openness (C-F-C-R-O). The events and artifacts are the skeleton; these values are the muscle that make them actually work.

Worked exampleA Retro only works if the values are alive: courage to say "our releases keep breaking because we skip testing," openness to admit it, and respect so it's a systemic conversation, not a blame session. Without the values, a Retro is just a quiet meeting where nothing real is said.
Q17

What is the Scrum Team?

One team of typically 10 or fewer people with three accountabilities: Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers. There are no sub-teams or hierarchies — they share one Product Goal and are collectively accountable for creating value each Sprint.

Worked exampleOur booking team is 7 people: 1 PO (prioritises what patients/clinics need most), 1 Scrum Master (me), and 5 Developers (including 2 who focus on testing and one on integration with the clinic system). No "team lead" hands out tasks — the five Developers plan the work together.
Q18

What do "cross-functional" and "self-managing" mean?

Cross-functional = the team has all the skills needed to build a "Done" increment without waiting on outsiders. Self-managing = the team decides who does what and how, rather than being directed.

Worked exampleA cross-functional booking team can design, build, test and deploy a feature itself. If every release has to queue for a separate QA team and a separate DBA team, it's not truly cross-functional — those hand-offs are exactly the impediments a Scrum Master works to remove.
Q19

What is the ideal Scrum team size?

The 2020 Guide says 10 or fewer. Small enough to stay nimble and communicate easily, large enough to complete meaningful work each Sprint. If it grows too big, you split into multiple cohesive Scrum Teams sharing one Product Goal and one Product Backlog.

Worked exampleWhen our platform team hit 14 people, Daily Scrums dragged and coordination got messy. We split into a "Booking" team and a "Telehealth" team — each under 8 — sharing one Product Goal and PO. Communication and predictability both improved immediately.
Q20

What is a Sprint?

A fixed, short cycle (1–4 weeks) in which the team builds a usable increment. It's the heartbeat of Scrum — a new Sprint starts the moment the last one ends, giving a steady, predictable rhythm. The length stays consistent, quality never drops, and the Sprint Goal doesn't change mid-Sprint.

Everyday analogy Like a season of a TV show — a fixed length that tells a complete, watchable story, after which the writers learn from the audience before planning the next season.
Q21

Can the Sprint length change?

Keep it consistent for predictability, but a team can deliberately change its standard Sprint length between Sprints (e.g. move from 3 weeks to 2) if it helps. What you must never do is extend a Sprint mid-flight just to "finish" work.

Worked exampleOur team moved from 3-week to 2-week Sprints because clinic feedback was arriving too slowly. That's a fine, deliberate change. But when a release looked like it might miss the last day, we did not tack on "just two more days" — the unfinished item simply returned to the backlog for the next Sprint.
Q22

What is the Sprint Goal?

A single, concise objective for the Sprint that gives the team focus and a shared "why." It's the one commitment attached to the Sprint Backlog. Individual items can flex, but the Goal stays fixed for the Sprint.

Worked exampleA weak goal is "finish these 8 tickets." A strong Sprint Goal is "patients can book, reschedule and cancel an appointment online for one pilot clinic." Now if one ticket turns out huge, the team can drop or swap work while still delivering the goal — the tickets serve the goal, not the other way round.
Q23

What is the Product Goal?

The long-term objective for the product — a future state the team is working toward. It's the commitment attached to the Product Backlog, and every Sprint Goal should move the product a step closer to it. Introduced in the 2020 Scrum Guide to give the backlog a clear "north star."

Worked exampleProduct Goal: "Become the easiest way for patients to manage all their appointments across every clinic in the network." Sprint Goals then ladder up to it — online booking, then reminders, then telehealth, then multi-clinic view — each a stepping stone toward that bigger destination.
Q24

Is Scrum only for software?

No. Scrum was popularised in software but works for any complex, adaptive problem where requirements emerge — marketing campaigns, hardware, research, even event planning. The Scrum Guide is intentionally domain-agnostic; only the practices you layer on top (like TDD) are software-specific.

Worked exampleA hospital's patient-experience team used Scrum to redesign its admissions process — Sprints of small changes, fortnightly reviews with nurses, retros to improve — with not a line of code involved. The framework fit because the problem was complex and feedback-driven.
03 Roles & Accountabilities

Who's who on a Scrum Team

The 2020 Guide calls these accountabilities, not "roles." There are exactly three.

Three accountabilities, one team, one shared goal.

Q25

What are the three accountabilities in Scrum?

  • Product Owner — maximises value; owns and orders the Product Backlog (the what & why).
  • Developers — build the increment (the how); anyone doing the work — coders, testers, designers.
  • Scrum Master — accountable for the team's effectiveness and the correct use of Scrum (the how we work).
Worked exampleOn our booking team: the PO decides "reminders before booking a multi-clinic view" (priority); the Developers decide whether to use SMS or push and how to build it; and I, the SM, make sure the team can actually deliver — clearing a blocked test environment, keeping the Review useful. Three lanes, one goal.
Q26

What does a Scrum Master do?

A servant leader and coach who makes the team and its environment better — never a task-master. They serve three groups: the team (coach self-management, remove impediments, facilitate events), the Product Owner (backlog and goal help, stakeholder collaboration), and the organisation (help everyone adopt Scrum, remove barriers between teams and stakeholders).

Everyday analogy A sports coach — not the captain, not a player. They don't score the goals; they train the players, study the opponents, clear obstacles, and help the team get better every game.
Worked exampleA typical week for me: unblock a delayed API from the integrations team, coach the PO on slicing a huge "telehealth" epic, facilitate the Retro, and spend an afternoon helping a stakeholder understand why dumping work straight on a developer hurts delivery. Barely any of it is "running meetings."
Q27

Scrum Master vs Project Manager?

A PM directs from above — owns scope, budget and timeline, and assigns tasks. A Scrum Master serves from alongside — owns the process and team health, has no command authority, and leads through influence and coaching. The team pulls its own work and is collectively accountable for delivery.

Worked exampleWhen a booking feature slipped, a PM might reassign people and push a new deadline. As SM, I instead facilitated the team to find the root cause (a hidden dependency), helped them re-plan, and coached the PO on renegotiating scope with the clinic — the team owned the fix, I enabled it.
★
Say this "A PM pushes work down and manages the plan; a Scrum Master creates the conditions for a self-managing team to succeed — leadership through influence, not authority."
Q28

What is servant leadership?

Leading by serving the team's needs first — asking "what does the team need to succeed, and what's in the way?" instead of giving orders. In practice: clearing blockers, shielding focus, asking questions rather than dictating answers, and building the team's confidence to decide for itself. The 2020 Guide frames the SM as "a true leader who serves."

Worked exampleA developer asked me "which caching approach should we use?" A directive leader answers. As a servant leader I said, "What options have you weighed, and what does the team think?" — then removed the real blocker (getting them 30 minutes with the architect). They made a better decision and grew.
Q29

What does the Product Owner do?

A single person accountable for maximising the product's value. They own the Product Backlog and its ordering, set the Product Goal, represent stakeholders and patients/customers, and decide what gets built and in what order — but not how, and not how much the team commits to per Sprint.

Worked exampleOur PO chose to build appointment reminders before a fancy calendar view, because no-show data showed reminders would cut missed appointments most. That's the PO's core job: turning limited capacity into maximum value, and defending that ordering to stakeholders.
★
Key point One person, not a committee — and for the PO to work, the organisation must respect their decisions.
Q30

What are Developers responsible for?

Creating the Sprint Backlog, building a "Done" increment that meets the Definition of Done, adapting their daily plan toward the Sprint Goal, and holding each other accountable as professionals. "Developers" means anyone building the increment — coders, testers, designers, analysts.

Worked exampleIn our team, the two people who mostly write automated tests are "Developers" too. When we plan a booking story, they shape the acceptance tests up front — so quality is the whole team's job, not a separate phase handed off at the end.
Q31

Scrum Master vs Agile Coach?

A Scrum Master focuses on one or two teams and the Scrum framework. An Agile Coach works more broadly — multiple teams, leadership, and the organisation's overall agile transformation and culture — and is often framework-agnostic. The SM role is the common stepping stone to coaching.

Worked exampleAs SM I help my booking team run great Sprints. Our Agile Coach operates a level up — running maturity assessments across all five teams on the train, coaching directors on Lean budgeting, and fixing systemic issues like the slow release-approval process that no single team can solve alone.
Q32

Can one person be both Scrum Master and Product Owner?

Strongly discouraged — the roles can conflict. The PO pushes for more scope and value; the SM protects the team, quality and sustainable pace. One person wearing both hats loses that healthy tension and objectivity. It happens in small startups, but you should name it as a risk.

Worked exampleIf I were both, and the clinic demanded more features than we could safely build, "PO me" would want to say yes while "SM me" should protect the team from overload — I'd have no one to hold that balance. That's exactly why Scrum separates them.
Q33

Can a Scrum Master serve multiple teams?

Yes — experienced SMs often serve 2–3 teams. It depends on team maturity: a newly formed team needs far more attention than a mature, self-managing one. The risk is spreading so thin you become a passive "meeting scheduler" instead of an active coach.

Worked exampleI comfortably support two mature teams. But when we spun up a brand-new telehealth team, I flagged that it needed near-full-time coaching for its first couple of PIs — and pushed back on being handed a third team, because that would have meant only booking meetings, not actually coaching.
Q34

Who manages the team in Scrum?

No one "manages" it in the traditional command sense — the team is self-managing. There's no team lead or manager inside Scrum. The Scrum Master facilitates and coaches; line management (HR, careers, pay) usually sits outside the Scrum Team.

Worked exampleNew joiners often ask me "so who assigns my tasks?" The answer: no one — you pull the next priority item off the board with the team. It surprises people used to a boss handing out work, and coaching that shift is a big part of the SM role.
Q35

What is the Scrum Master's role with the wider organisation?

Helping the organisation understand and adopt Scrum, coaching leaders and stakeholders, removing cross-team barriers, and driving an empirical, agile culture. It's the most overlooked part of the role — and what separates a senior SM from a ceremony-runner.

Worked exampleOur releases kept stalling on a multi-day manual compliance sign-off — an organisational impediment no single team could fix. I gathered the data, showed leadership the delay in the value stream, and got the review moved earlier and run in parallel. That's org-level Scrum Mastery.
Q36

What makes a good Scrum Master?

  • Strong facilitation and coaching skills; asks more than tells.
  • Empathy and emotional intelligence; builds trust and psychological safety.
  • Servant leadership — influence without authority.
  • Deep Scrum knowledge, plus conflict resolution and impediment removal.
  • Aims to make themselves less needed over time.
Worked exampleThe best compliment I've had was a team saying, "we barely notice you're gone for a day" — because they'd learned to run their own events, resolve their own blockers, and hold each other accountable. A great SM builds a team that eventually needs them little.
04 Scrum Events

The Sprint and its four ceremonies

Each event is a formal inspect-and-adapt opportunity, and each is timeboxed.

The Sprint is itself an event — a container holding the other four.

Q37

What are the five Scrum events?

  1. The Sprint — the container for all others (1–4 weeks).
  2. Sprint Planning — plan the Sprint (max 8h for a 1-month Sprint).
  3. Daily Scrum — 15-min daily sync for Developers.
  4. Sprint Review — show the increment, get feedback (max 4h).
  5. Sprint Retrospective — improve how the team works (max 3h).
Q38

What is Sprint Planning?

The event where the whole team plans the upcoming Sprint — crafting the Sprint Goal, selecting backlog items, and making a plan to deliver them. Output: the Sprint Backlog. Timebox: up to 8 hours for a one-month Sprint (less for shorter).

Q39

What three topics does Sprint Planning cover?

  1. Why is this Sprint valuable? → the Sprint Goal.
  2. What can be done this Sprint? → selected backlog items.
  3. How will the work get done? → the plan / breakdown.
Q40

What is the Daily Scrum?

A 15-minute daily event for the Developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and re-plan the day. Same time, same place. The old "three questions" are now optional — any format works as long as it drives toward the Goal.

Q41

Is the Daily Scrum a status meeting for the manager/SM?

▲
Trap No! It's by and for the Developers to talk to each other, not a report-up. The SM ensures it happens but doesn't run it or collect status.
Q42

What is the Sprint Review?

At the Sprint's end, the team shows the working increment to stakeholders, gathers feedback, and discusses what's next. It's a working session, not a one-way demo — the backlog is often adjusted based on what's learned. Timebox: up to 4 hours for a one-month Sprint.

Q43

What is the Sprint Retrospective?

The team's private session to inspect how it worked (people, process, tools) and pick concrete improvements for next Sprint. Often called the most important event — it's the engine of continuous improvement. Timebox: up to 3 hours.

Q44

Review vs Retrospective — what's the difference?

The Review inspects the product (with stakeholders); the Retrospective inspects the team and process (team only). Review = "what did we build?"; Retro = "how can we work better?"

Q45

What is timeboxing?

Giving an activity a fixed maximum duration — when time's up, you stop. It creates focus and prevents endless meetings. Scrum events have a maximum, not a required length — an 8-minute Daily is fine.

Q46

Is Backlog Refinement a Scrum event?

No — refinement is an ongoing activity, not one of the five formal events. It's the continuous work of adding detail, estimates, and order to backlog items so they're ready for future Sprints. Typically ~10% of the team's capacity.

Q47

Who attends each event?

  • Sprint Planning / Retro: the whole Scrum Team.
  • Daily Scrum: the Developers (SM/PO may attend if working on Sprint items).
  • Sprint Review: the Scrum Team plus stakeholders.
Q48

Can Scrum events be skipped or combined?

No — skipping events removes inspect-and-adapt opportunities and breaks empiricism. If an event feels useless, that's a signal to improve it (in the retro), not drop it. The Sprint is the only container; the other four all happen every Sprint.

Q49

Can there be a gap between Sprints?

No. A new Sprint starts immediately after the previous one ends. Continuous cadence is the point — no "sprint zero gaps" or breaks for planning.

Q50

What is a "Sprint 0" — is it real Scrum?

"Sprint 0" (setup/prep before real delivery) is a common practice but not part of Scrum. The Scrum Guide expects every Sprint to produce a valuable increment. Setup work is fine, just don't pretend it's a formal Scrum concept.

05 Artifacts & Commitments

The three things Scrum makes visible

Each artifact has a commitment attached that gives it focus.

Q51

What are the three Scrum artifacts?

  • Product Backlog — the ordered list of everything the product might need.
  • Sprint Backlog — the Sprint Goal + selected items + the plan.
  • Increment — a usable, "Done" step toward the Product Goal.
Q52

What is the Product Backlog?

The single, ordered source of truth for all work the product might need — features, fixes, improvements — most valuable at the top. Owned by the Product Owner. It's dynamic and never "complete."

Q53

What is the Sprint Backlog?

The Developers' plan for the current Sprint: the Sprint Goal, the items selected, and how they'll be built. Owned by the Developers, updated throughout the Sprint as they learn.

Q54

What is the Increment?

A concrete, usable step toward the Product Goal that meets the Definition of Done. Multiple increments can be created in a Sprint; each must be integrated and verified. An increment is only "Done" if it meets the DoD.

Q55

What are the three commitments?

Each artifact has one: Product Backlog → Product Goal, Sprint Backlog → Sprint Goal, Increment → Definition of Done. They add transparency and keep each artifact focused on value and quality.

Q56

What is the Definition of Done (DoD)?

A shared quality checklist a piece of work must meet to be truly finished — e.g. code reviewed, tested, documented, deployed to staging. If it doesn't meet the DoD, it is not Done and can't be part of the increment.

Everyday analogy A restaurant's "ready to serve" standard: plated, correct temperature, garnished, checked. A half-cooked dish isn't done just because the timer went off.
Q57

What is the Definition of Ready (DoR)? Is it official?

A checklist for when a backlog item is ready to be pulled into a Sprint (clear, estimated, dependency-free, has acceptance criteria). It's a useful practice but not part of the official Scrum Guide — unlike the DoD, which is.

Q58

What is the exit criteria for DoD and entry criteria for DoR?

DoR is the entry gate — criteria a story must meet to enter a Sprint. DoD is the exit gate — criteria a story must meet to leave as "Done." DoR guards the start; DoD guards the finish.

Q59

What is backlog refinement (grooming)?

The ongoing activity of breaking down, detailing, estimating and re-ordering backlog items so upcoming ones are ready. It keeps the top of the backlog healthy. Not a formal event; typically ~10% of capacity.

Q60

What is backlog ordering based on?

Whatever maximises value — commonly business value, risk, dependencies, cost of delay, and effort. The PO decides the order. Techniques like MoSCoW or WSJF help, but the PO makes the final call.

Q61

What is "Backlog Health"?

How ready and well-ordered the backlog is: top items are refined, sized, and have acceptance criteria (roughly 2–3 sprints ready ahead), lower items stay coarse. A healthy backlog makes planning smooth and predictable.

Q62

What does "potentially shippable" mean?

The increment is of releasable quality — it could go live at any point (it meets the DoD), even if the business chooses not to release it yet. Quality is built in every Sprint, not deferred.

06 User Stories & Estimation

Describing and sizing the work

Not strictly Scrum terms (they come from XP), but every interview expects them.

Q63

What is a user story?

A short, plain-language description of a feature from the user's point of view. The common template:

"As a [user], I want [goal] so that [benefit]."

Q64

What are the 3 C's of a user story?

  • Card — the short written story (the placeholder).
  • Conversation — the discussion that fleshes out the detail.
  • Confirmation — the acceptance criteria that confirm it's done.
★
Follow-up they ask "If developers still have lots of questions about a story, which C is missing?" → the Conversation.
Q65

What is INVEST?

The qualities of a good user story: Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, Testable.

★
Follow-up "If a story is valuable, estimable and testable but can't be done without another story, which is missing?" → Independent. (Most commonly violated: Independent or Small.)
Q66

What are acceptance criteria?

The specific conditions a story must satisfy to be accepted — the "confirmation." Often written as scenarios (Given / When / Then). They make "done" objective and testable, and they scope the story.

Q67

Epic vs Feature vs Story vs Task?

  • Epic — a large body of work spanning many sprints ("online banking system").
  • Feature — a sizeable capability, fits within a PI ("fund transfers").
  • Story — a small, user-valued slice, fits in a sprint ("transfer to a saved payee").
  • Task — a technical to-do inside a story ("add validation").
★
Follow-up "'Build an online banking system' — story, feature or epic?" → an Epic (far too big for one sprint).
Q68

What are story points?

A relative measure of effort, complexity and uncertainty — not hours. Teams size items against each other, often on a Fibonacci-like scale (1,2,3,5,8,13…).

Everyday analogy You may not know exact minutes to climb a hill, but you can say "this hill is about twice as hard as that one." Relative size is easier and more reliable to judge than exact time.
Q69

Why estimate in points instead of hours?

Because relative sizing is more reliable than absolute time, it's less personal (a 5 is a 5 regardless of who does it), it accounts for complexity and uncertainty — not just effort — and it removes the false precision and pressure of hour estimates.

Q70

What is Planning Poker?

A consensus estimation game: each developer privately picks a card (Fibonacci value), all reveal at once, and outliers explain their reasoning. It surfaces hidden assumptions and reduces anchoring bias, then the team converges.

Q71

Why the Fibonacci sequence for estimating?

The growing gaps (1,2,3,5,8,13…) reflect rising uncertainty in bigger items — you can't meaningfully tell an "11" from a "12," so the scale forces a choice and discourages false precision on large stories.

Q72

What is velocity?

The average story points a team completes per Sprint, used for forecasting. It's a planning aid for one team — never a productivity target or a way to compare teams.

Q73

What is capacity planning?

Working out how much a team can realistically take on in a Sprint given actual availability — leave, holidays, part-time, support duties, meetings — rather than just using raw velocity. It prevents over-commitment.

Q74

What is a spike?

A time-boxed research task to reduce uncertainty — you build knowledge (or a throwaway prototype) rather than shippable functionality. Used when a story is too unclear or risky to estimate.

Q75

What are the types of spikes?

Technical spike — explore a technical approach (which library, is it feasible, performance?). Functional spike — explore how users would interact / clarify requirements. There are also research and refactoring spikes; all are time-boxed to protect the Sprint.

Q76

What is MVP vs MMF?

MVP (Minimum Viable Product) — the smallest release that delivers value and lets you learn from real users. MMF (Minimum Marketable Feature) — the smallest feature chunk worth releasing to market. MVP is about validated learning; MMF is about marketable value.

07 Metrics & Tracking

Making progress and flow visible

Know what each chart shows — and its limits. Metrics inform conversations, they aren't targets.

Animated

Burndown — work remaining should trend to zero by the Sprint's end. Above the ideal line = behind.

Q77

What is a burndown chart?

A chart of work remaining over time, trending toward zero by the end of the Sprint (or release). Comparing the actual line to the ideal line shows whether you're ahead or behind. It's a conversation-starter, not a scoreboard.

Q78

What is a burnup chart?

The mirror image — it tracks work completed rising toward a total. Its advantage: a separate "total scope" line makes scope changes (scope creep) clearly visible, which a burndown hides.

Q79

Burndown vs burnup — when to use which?

Use burndown for a quick "are we on track this Sprint?" view. Use burnup when scope changes and you want to see completed work and total scope separately — better for releases and stakeholder communication.

Q80

What is a Cumulative Flow Diagram (CFD)?

A stacked area chart showing how many items are in each workflow state (To Do / In Progress / Done) over time. Widening bands = a bottleneck; a growing gap means WIP is piling up. Great for spotting where flow stalls.

Q81

What is cycle time?

The time from when the team starts working on an item to when it's done. Shorter cycle time = faster delivery. A core flow metric in Kanban and increasingly in Scrum.

Q82

What is lead time?

The time from when an item is requested/created to when it's delivered — the customer's-eye view. Lead time includes waiting time before work starts; cycle time doesn't. Lead time ≥ cycle time.

Q83

What is throughput?

The number of items completed per unit of time (e.g. 8 stories/sprint). A flow-based alternative to velocity that doesn't rely on story points.

Q84

What's the difference between WIP and throughput?

WIP = how many items are in progress right now. Throughput = how many you finish per period. Little's Law links them: lower WIP → shorter cycle time → smoother throughput. Limiting WIP is how you speed delivery.

Q85

What is a control chart?

A chart plotting each item's cycle time with a rolling average and control limits. It reveals consistency and outliers — helping you forecast and spot items that took abnormally long.

Q86

How do you measure whether a team is successful?

By outcomes, not output: value delivered to customers, predictability (meeting goals), quality (low escaped defects), team health, and continuous improvement. A team shipping lots of unused features isn't succeeding.

Q87

What is the say/do ratio?

Commitment reliability — work completed ÷ work committed per Sprint. It shows how trustworthy the team's forecasts are. Consistently high (85%+) matters more than raw velocity.

Q88

Is rising velocity always good?

▲
Trap No. Rising velocity can just mean inflated estimates, not more value. Pushing for "higher velocity" gamifies points. Watch stability and value delivered, not an ever-climbing number.
08 Scrum Master Craft

Impediments, facilitation, coaching, anti-patterns

The day-to-day of the role — where they test whether you actually do it, not just define it.

Q89

What is an impediment? Give examples.

Anything slowing or blocking the team from reaching its goal. Examples: a broken build/environment, a slow external approval, an unavailable dependency, unclear requirements, too many meetings, or a noisy stakeholder. The SM's job is to remove or escalate them.

Q90

How do you remove impediments?

  1. Make it visible — capture it (impediment log / board).
  2. Understand the root cause — don't just treat symptoms.
  3. Resolve what you can directly; coach the team to resolve its own where possible.
  4. Escalate organisational blockers to the right people (RTE, management).
  5. Track and follow up until it's genuinely cleared.
Q91

"What's the biggest impediment you've removed?" (behavioural)

Pick a real, specific example with a result — avoid the generic "a dependency." Use the shape: situation → what was blocked → what you did → measurable outcome.

★
Example shape "Releases were stalling on a multi-day manual approval. I mapped the flow, showed the wait to leadership, and got the review moved earlier and run in parallel — cutting release lead time by ~40%."
Q92

What is facilitation?

Guiding a group to a productive outcome without imposing your own — setting the structure, keeping it on track and inclusive, drawing out quiet voices, and helping the group decide. The SM facilitates events; they don't dominate them.

Q93

What are common Scrum anti-patterns?

  • Daily Scrum as a status report to the SM/manager.
  • The SM acting as a secretary/task-master instead of a coach.
  • Carrying over unfinished work sprint after sprint.
  • Skipping retros — or retros with no follow-through.
  • A proxy PO with no real authority; changing the Sprint Goal mid-sprint.
  • "Water-Scrum-fall" — Scrum ceremonies bolted onto a waterfall mindset.
Q94

How do you handle a team resistant to Scrum?

Get curious about why — resistance usually hides a real concern. Explain the benefit to them (not "because the framework says so"), start small, involve them in shaping how Scrum is applied, and let early wins build buy-in. Coach, don't force.

Q95

How do you coach a new / disengaged Product Owner?

Teach the value of the role, pair with them on backlog refinement and ordering, help them connect work to outcomes, and protect their decision authority with stakeholders. If they skip Scrum events, show them what breaks when they're absent (unclear priorities, rework) rather than nagging.

Q96

How do you build psychological safety?

Model vulnerability, make it safe to raise problems and admit mistakes, run blameless retros, ensure every voice is heard, and never punish honesty. Safety is the foundation for courage and openness — without it, retros go silent.

Q97

How do you know a team has become mature?

They self-organise and resolve most impediments themselves, deliver predictably with good quality, run their own events well, hold each other accountable, and improve continuously — needing the SM less and less. That's success, not redundancy.

Q98

How do you handle conflict within the team?

Healthy conflict about ideas is good. For personal conflict: facilitate a conversation, keep it on the work and shared goals, hear all sides, and help them resolve it themselves rather than imposing a verdict. Escalate only if it becomes a serious HR issue.

Q99

Coaching vs mentoring vs teaching vs facilitating?

  • Teaching — imparting knowledge ("here's how DoD works").
  • Mentoring — sharing your own experience/advice.
  • Coaching — asking questions so they find the answer.
  • Facilitating — guiding a group to its own outcome.

A great SM fluidly switches between all four ("stances").

Q100

What does a typical day look like for a Scrum Master?

Facilitate the Daily Scrum; follow up on impediments; support refinement; coach the PO and team; check board/flow health; prepare or run upcoming events; coordinate dependencies with other teams; and spend time on organisational improvement. No two days identical — but always in service of the team.

09 Scenario & Behavioural

"What would you do if…"

These test judgment. The winning pattern nearly always: get curious about the root cause → be transparent → coach and facilitate rather than command → make it visible.

Q101

A team member dominates every Daily Scrum. What do you do?

Observe first, then coach privately — never scold publicly. Have a quiet 1-on-1, reinforce that the Daily is for the whole team, introduce light structure (walk the board, timebox each person, "take it offline"), and raise it in the retro as a team norm if it persists.

Q102

The PO keeps changing priorities mid-Sprint. How do you handle it?

Protect the Sprint Goal — a core SM duty. Coach the PO on why stability within a Sprint matters, channel new ideas into the backlog for the next Sprint, and remind them the escape valve is cancelling the Sprint (PO-only) if the goal is truly obsolete — not constant churn.

Q103

The team consistently fails to finish committed work. What now?

Root-cause it in the retro, not blame. Are they over-committing? Coach capacity-based planning. Poor estimates? Improve refinement and slice smaller. Hidden interruptions? Make them visible. Vague DoD? Tighten it. Often the fix is commit to less and build trust in delivering it.

Q104

Two team members clash loudly in a meeting — one accuses another of breaking the build and you can't just mute them. How do you handle it?

Stay calm, acknowledge the frustration, and de-escalate in the moment — "this is clearly important; let's park it and the three of us will dig in right after." Take it to a private space, hear both sides, focus on the process gap (why did a broken check-in reach others — missing CI, no branch protection?), and turn it into a systemic fix, not a personal verdict.

Q105

Stakeholders bypass the PO and assign work directly to Developers. What do you do?

Coach stakeholders that all work flows through the backlog and the PO, empower developers to redirect such asks politely, and make the impact visible — side-work steals capacity from the agreed Sprint Goal.

Q106

The team is disengaged in retrospectives. How do you fix it?

Vary the format, make it psychologically safe, and — most importantly — follow through on past actions. If people never see change come out of retros, they stop investing. Occasionally step out so they can speak freely.

Q107

A developer estimated a story at 3 points; two days in they say it's 5. What do you do?

That's normal — estimates are forecasts, not contracts. I don't "re-bill" it mid-sprint; we note the learning, check whether the Sprint Goal is at risk, and if so re-plan with the PO (drop a lower-priority item). I feed the insight into better refinement next time, not pressure.

Q108

The team committed to 80 points but delivered 60. How do you handle and communicate it?

First, understand why in the retro (blockers, scope, over-commitment). Communicate to management transparently and early: what was delivered, why the gap, and the corrective action — framed around the Sprint Goal and value, not just a points miss. Then adjust future forecasting to be realistic.

Q109

The team has been working overtime for several Sprints. What do you do?

Unsustainable pace is a red flag I act on. Surface it with data (overtime, defects, morale), find the cause (over-commitment, understaffing, unrealistic dates), and address it — cut scope, reset expectations with stakeholders, protect a sustainable pace. Burnout destroys quality and predictability.

Q110

A cross-team dependency is delayed and your team is fully blocked. What do you do?

Escalate the dependency via the Scrum of Scrums / RTE immediately, pull forward other valuable backlog work to keep the team productive, use a mock/stub to the agreed interface if possible, and re-plan the Sprint Goal with the PO if the block is material. Then address why it wasn't caught at planning.

Q111

UAT is progressing slower than expected before a release. What do you do?

Make the risk visible early, re-prioritise UAT on the highest-risk / highest-value items first, add help where possible, and give the PO a clear picture to decide: descope, use a feature toggle, or move the date. Protect quality — don't rubber-stamp untested work to hit a date.

Q112

Critical production issues arise and the team must stop everything to fix them during a release. What do you do?

Patient/customer-impacting production issues take priority. Support the team in triaging and fixing, communicate the impact on Sprint commitments to the PO and stakeholders transparently, and afterward run an RCA so the same class of issue is prevented. Adjust the Sprint plan for the lost capacity.

Q113

A customer requests an immediate release. How do you evaluate and respond?

Assess with the PO: what's the value/urgency, is a shippable increment ready and DoD-met, what's the risk, and what's the cost to the current Sprint. If justified, release the smallest safe slice (ideally we already have a potentially-shippable increment). If not, be honest about the trade-off rather than shipping something risky.

Q114

The business wants to avoid writing detailed requirements up front. How do you handle evolving requirements?

That's actually Agile-friendly — we embrace emergent requirements. Use lightweight stories with just-in-time detail via the "conversation," strong ongoing refinement, and frequent demos so the product evolves with feedback. The key is a shared Product Goal and a healthy backlog, not a big up-front spec.

Q115

The business keeps requesting UI changes after every demo. How do you control scope while staying Agile?

Feedback is welcome — but it goes into the backlog, prioritised by the PO, not straight into the running Sprint. Distinguish genuine learning from endless polishing, set acceptance criteria and a "good enough" bar with the PO, and use the Sprint boundary to keep changes controlled.

Q116

New scope keeps getting added during Sprint execution. What do you do?

Protect the Sprint Goal — new scope generally waits for the next Sprint. If something is truly urgent, the PO trades it in by removing equivalent work (capacity is fixed). Track the churn and raise it in the retro; persistent mid-sprint additions are a sign the intake process needs fixing.

Q117

The PO gave stakeholders a deadline without consulting the team. How do you handle it?

Coach the PO that only the team can forecast how much fits in the time. Use velocity/capacity data to reality-check the deadline, then facilitate a conversation with stakeholders about scope vs date. Aim to rebuild the habit of the PO and team forecasting together.

Q118

The CEO says "stop following Scrum from tomorrow." How do you respond in 2–3 minutes? And if they still refuse?

Don't argue dogma — connect to their goals: "Scrum is how we get predictable delivery, fast feedback and quality. Which outcome is it failing to give you?" Address the real pain (maybe it's bad implementation, not Scrum). If they still refuse: a Scrum Master can't force Scrum — I'd respect the decision, help transition responsibly, and keep the valuable practices (short feedback loops, transparency) whatever we call them.

★
Related trap "Can a Scrum Master force Scrum?" → No. The SM leads by influence, not authority. Adoption must be chosen, not mandated by the SM.
10 SAFe & Scaling

Agile beyond a single team

Mid-to-senior interviews probe how Scrum stretches across many teams — SAFe terms come up constantly.

An ART aligns several teams to a shared cadence and PI; the last iteration is the Innovation & Planning (IP) iteration.

Q119

What is SAFe?

The Scaled Agile Framework — a set of patterns for applying Lean-Agile across many teams, programs and the portfolio. It's the most widely adopted (and most prescriptive) scaling framework, organising teams into Agile Release Trains.

Q120

What is an Agile Release Train (ART)?

A long-lived team of Agile teams (typically 5–12 teams, 50–125 people) that plans, commits and delivers together on a shared cadence (the PI). It's the primary value-delivery vehicle in SAFe.

Q121

What is a Program Increment (PI)?

A timebox (~8–12 weeks) in which an ART delivers value, made up of several iterations plus a final Innovation & Planning (IP) iteration. A PI is to an ART what a Sprint is to a team.

Q122

What is PI Planning?

A two-day event where the whole ART plans the upcoming PI together — aligning on vision, drafting team plans iteration-by-iteration, surfacing dependencies on the program board, setting PI objectives, ROAMing risks, and finishing with a confidence vote. It's the heartbeat of SAFe alignment.

Q123

What is the Release Train Engineer (RTE)?

The "Scrum Master of the ART" — a servant leader who facilitates ART events (PI Planning, Scrum of Scrums, I&A), manages risks and dependencies across teams, and drives relentless improvement at the program level.

Q124

Scrum Master vs RTE?

Same servant-leadership pattern, different scope: the SM serves one team; the RTE serves the whole ART (many teams). The RTE coordinates the SMs and handles program-level impediments and dependencies.

Q125

What is WSJF?

Weighted Shortest Job First — a prioritisation model: WSJF = Cost of Delay ÷ Job Size. Cost of Delay = user/business value + time criticality + risk reduction/opportunity. You do the highest-WSJF items first to maximise value delivered. Used to order the program backlog.

Q126

What is the confidence vote?

At the end of PI Planning, everyone votes fist-of-five on their confidence in meeting the PI objectives. Average 3+ proceeds; below 3, the plan is reworked. Low individual votes must be heard.

Q127

What is ROAM?

How program risks are handled at PI Planning: Resolved (no longer a concern), Owned (someone owns it), Accepted (can't fix, live with it), Mitigated (plan to reduce it). Every risk is openly ROAMed.

Q128

Committed vs uncommitted (stretch) PI objectives?

Committed objectives are high-confidence and count toward the PI Predictability measure. Uncommitted (stretch) are lower-certainty — planned and visible, giving a buffer, but not counted in the commitment (they count in actuals if delivered). They protect predictability while keeping stretch work visible.

Q129

What is PI Predictability?

Actual business value delivered ÷ planned business value (committed objectives), scored by Business Owners at the end of the PI. A healthy team/ART lands 80–100%. It's the headline SAFe reliability metric.

Q130

What is the Inspect & Adapt (I&A) event?

The ART-level retrospective at the end of each PI: a PI System Demo, quantitative/qualitative measurement, and a structured problem-solving workshop that produces improvement backlog items for the next PI. Think "retrospective for the whole train."

Q131

What is the System Demo?

A demo of the integrated work of all teams on the ART, ideally every iteration. Unlike a single team's Sprint Review, it shows the full system working together — the real measure of ART progress.

Q132

SAFe vs LeSS vs Nexus vs Scrum of Scrums?

  • Scrum of Scrums — a simple sync meeting to coordinate dependencies across teams.
  • Nexus — Scrum.org's lightweight framework for ~3–9 teams; adds a Nexus Integration Team.
  • LeSS — Large-Scale Scrum; keeps Scrum almost as-is with one PO and one backlog across teams; minimal extra process.
  • SAFe — the heaviest/most prescriptive; adds program & portfolio layers. Popular in large enterprises.

Principle is common: align teams to one goal while managing dependencies. Pick the lightest that solves the problem.

11 Kanban & Flow

Scrum's continuous-flow cousin

Expect a Scrum-vs-Kanban question in almost every interview.

Animated

Kanban = continuous pull. The card is pulled across only when there's capacity (WIP limit) — no fixed sprints.

Q133

What is Kanban?

An Agile method based on visualising work, limiting work-in-progress (WIP), and managing continuous flow — pulling new work only as capacity frees up. No fixed iterations or prescribed roles.

Q134

Scrum vs Kanban?

  • Cadence: Scrum = fixed sprints; Kanban = continuous flow.
  • Roles/events: Scrum prescribes them; Kanban doesn't.
  • Change: Scrum between sprints; Kanban any time.
  • Core lever: Scrum = sprint commitment; Kanban = WIP limits.
Q135

What is Scrumban?

A hybrid — Scrum's structure with Kanban's flow and WIP limits. Popular for maintenance/support teams with unpredictable, interrupt-driven work that still want some cadence and ceremonies.

Q136

What are WIP limits?

Caps on how many items can be in a workflow state at once. They expose bottlenecks, force finishing over starting, and shorten cycle time. "Stop starting, start finishing."

Q137

What is a pull system?

Work is pulled into the next stage only when there's capacity — rather than pushed based on a schedule. It prevents overloading and keeps flow smooth. Kanban is a pull system.

Q138

What is Little's Law?

Cycle Time = WIP ÷ Throughput. Practically: the more items you have in progress at once, the longer each takes to finish. It's the maths behind limiting WIP to go faster.

Q139

When would you use Kanban over Scrum?

When work is continuous and unpredictable — support, ops, BAU, incident response — where fixed sprint commitments don't fit. Also good for very mature teams that flow well without sprint structure, or as a first step for teams not ready for full Scrum.

Q140

Can you use WSJF or flow metrics in Scrum?

Yes — they're complementary. Many Scrum teams use WSJF to order the backlog and track flow metrics (cycle time, throughput, CFD) alongside velocity. Scrum doesn't forbid borrowing good practices.

12 Engineering & Quality

Technical practices a Scrum Master should know

You won't code, but you must understand these to coach quality and remove technical impediments.

Q141

What is technical debt?

The future cost of shortcuts taken today — quick or messy code that will slow the team later, like interest on a loan. Some is deliberate and fine; unmanaged debt compounds and kills velocity.

Q142

How do you handle technical debt as a Scrum Master?

Make it visible (as backlog items), coach a strong DoD to stop new debt forming, and help the PO understand why reserving some capacity each sprint to pay it down protects future delivery. Frame it in business terms — speed and risk — not just "clean code."

Q143

What is refactoring?

Improving the internal structure of code without changing its external behaviour — making it cleaner and easier to maintain. It's how teams keep technical debt under control continuously.

Q144

What is Test-Driven Development (TDD)?

Writing the test first, then just enough code to pass it, then refactoring — the "red-green-refactor" loop. It builds quality in from the start and produces a safety net of automated tests.

Q145

TDD vs Shift-Left testing?

Shift-left is the broad principle of testing earlier in the lifecycle (not just at the end). TDD is one specific practice that embodies it. Shift-left is the philosophy; TDD is a technique that delivers it.

Q146

What are CI/CD and DevOps?

CI (Continuous Integration) = merge and automatically build/test code frequently. CD (Continuous Delivery/Deployment) = keep it always releasable / auto-deploy. DevOps = the culture and tooling uniting development and operations for fast, reliable delivery. All enable the small, frequent, quality releases Agile promises.

13 AI for Scrum Masters

The modern-SM questions

Increasingly common. Be concrete and balanced — AI augments the role, it doesn't replace judgment.

Q147

How can AI help a Scrum Master?

  • Drafting user stories & acceptance criteria (then refined with the team).
  • Summarising retros/reviews into action items; generating status updates.
  • Analysing defect, spillover and cycle-time data for patterns.
  • Generating test-case outlines from acceptance criteria.

It saves time on admin/analysis so the SM spends more on coaching people.

Q148

How do you use AI in Jira?

Atlassian Intelligence offers natural-language JQL generation, summarising long issues/threads, drafting descriptions and acceptance criteria, and AI-assisted automation rules. Say what you've actually used and be aware of the rest.

Q149

What is Generative AI in an Agile context?

AI that creates content (text, code, images) from prompts. In Agile teams it accelerates drafting stories, docs, test cases and boilerplate code — a productivity assistant, with a human always reviewing the output.

Q150

What are the risks of using AI as a Scrum Master?

▲
Show balance Data privacy/confidentiality (never feed sensitive or customer/PHI data into public tools), over-reliance and skill atrophy, hallucinated/incorrect output, and losing the human touch. Always keep a human in the loop and validate.
14 Curveballs & Traps

The ones designed to catch you out

Bonus questions — logic puzzles and classic traps interviewers slip in to test how you think.

B1

How do you influence without authority?

Through trust, data, and relationships: build credibility by delivering, use transparent metrics to make trade-offs visible, appeal to shared goals, listen genuinely, and let the team/stakeholders reach the conclusion themselves. Influence is earned, not assigned — it's the core of the SM role.

B2

Logic puzzle: find the 3 fastest of 20 horses, 5 at a time, no stopwatch.

They're testing structured reasoning, not the exact answer. Talk it through: 5 races of 4 horses each (25 horses; for 20 it's fewer) → race the winners → then a final race of the strongest candidates. The classic answer for 25 horses is 7 races. Narrate your logic calmly; that's what they're scoring.

B3

Guesstimate: how many people board a train from Bangalore to a destination?

Again, show your method: coaches × seats per coach × occupancy, adjusted for time of day/route popularity. E.g. 20 coaches × ~70 seats × 80% ≈ 1,100. The number doesn't matter — a clear, reasonable breakdown does.

B4

Rapid-fire traps — the correct answers.

  • Who writes user stories? Anyone can; the PO is accountable for the backlog.
  • Can a Sprint be extended? Never. It can be cancelled (PO only) if the goal is obsolete.
  • Is the SM responsible if a Sprint fails? No single person — the team owns delivery collectively; Scrum promotes collective ownership over individual blame.
  • Does "individuals over processes" mean tools don't matter? No — if Jira is down, work continues on a whiteboard; tools support people, not the reverse.
  • Detailed story vs valuable story — which wins? A valuable story; detail emerges through conversation.

Final tip.
For factual questions, be crisp and use the right terms. For "what would you do" questions, narrate your thinking: acknowledge → get curious about the root cause → coach/facilitate rather than command → make it visible in the right event. That structure signals a real Scrum Master mindset, even for a scenario you've never seen.
