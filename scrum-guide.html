01 Scrum Fundamentals

What Scrum is and the thinking behind it

Start here. These are the "warm-up" questions almost every interview opens with — get them crisp and confident.

Q1

What is Agile, and how is Scrum related to it?

Agile is a mindset — a way of building products in small steps, getting feedback often, and adapting as you learn, instead of planning everything up front. It's captured in the Agile Manifesto (2001) and its 12 principles.

Scrum is the most popular framework for actually doing Agile. Agile is the "why and what"; Scrum is one concrete "how".

Everyday analogy Agile is like the idea of "eating healthily." Scrum is one specific meal plan that puts that idea into practice. You can be healthy without that exact plan — but the plan gives you clear structure.
★
Say this "Agile is a set of values and principles; Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps a team live those values through fixed roles, events, and artifacts."
Q2

What is Scrum in one clear sentence?

Scrum is a lightweight framework that helps teams deliver value in short, repeatable cycles called Sprints, by working together, inspecting results frequently, and adapting as they go.

"Lightweight" matters: the Scrum Guide is only about 13 pages. It deliberately tells you very little — it defines the minimum rules and leaves the rest to the team. It is a framework, not a step-by-step methodology.

★
Say this "Scrum is intentionally incomplete — it's a container for good practices, not a recipe. It surfaces problems fast so the team can adapt."
Q3

What are the three pillars of Scrum?

Scrum is built on empiricism — the belief that knowledge comes from experience and decisions from what we actually observe. It stands on three pillars:

No pillar stands alone — you can only adapt what you inspect, and you can only inspect what's transparent.

  • Transparency — the work and progress are visible and honest to everyone. (e.g. a shared board, a clear Definition of Done.)
  • Inspection — you regularly check the work and how you're working. (That's what the Scrum events are for.)
  • Adaptation — when inspection shows a problem, you adjust quickly rather than waiting.
Q4

What are the five Scrum values?

These are the behaviors that make the framework actually work. A common memory hook is C-F-C-R-O:

  • Commitment — the team commits to goals and to supporting each other.
  • Focus — everyone focuses on the Sprint's work and goal.
  • Courage — courage to tackle hard problems and say uncomfortable truths.
  • Respect — treat each other as capable, independent people.
  • Openness — be open about the work and the challenges.
★
Say this "The events and artifacts are the skeleton; the five values are the muscle that makes them move. Without courage and openness, a retrospective is just a quiet meeting."
Q5

What is a Sprint?

A Sprint is a fixed, short period — usually 1 to 4 weeks — during which the team builds a usable, valuable piece of the product. It's the heartbeat of Scrum: a new Sprint starts the moment the previous one ends, so the team has a steady rhythm.

Key rules: the length stays consistent, the Sprint Goal doesn't change mid-Sprint, and quality never drops.

Everyday analogy Think of a season of a TV show. Each season (Sprint) is a fixed length, tells a complete story you can actually watch (a usable increment), and the writers learn from audience reaction before planning the next season.
Q6

How is Agile/Scrum different from Waterfall?

Waterfall does everything once, in sequence — plan everything, then build everything, then test, then release at the very end. Scrum does a little of everything in every Sprint and releases value repeatedly.

Waterfall bets everything on one release far in the future. Scrum ships small, learns, and corrects course repeatedly.

★
Balanced answer Don't bash Waterfall. Say: "Waterfall works when requirements are stable and well understood. Scrum shines when there's uncertainty and change — which is most software."
02 Roles & Accountabilities

Who's who on a Scrum Team

Modern Scrum calls these accountabilities, not "roles." A Scrum Team has exactly three, and no sub-teams or hierarchies.

Three accountabilities, one team, one shared goal. The Scrum Master owns the way of working — not the people.

Q7

What are the three accountabilities on a Scrum Team?

  • Product Owner (PO) — owns the what and why. Maximises the value of the product; manages and orders the Product Backlog.
  • Developers — the people who do the work of building the increment (anyone building it: coders, testers, designers). They own the how.
  • Scrum Master — accountable for the team's effectiveness and for the correct use of Scrum. Owns how the team works together.

The team is cross-functional (has all skills needed) and self-managing (decides who does what and how).

Q8

What exactly does a Scrum Master do all day?

The Scrum Master is a servant leader and coach. They don't assign tasks or manage people — they make the team and the environment better. Concretely, they serve three groups:

  • The team — coach self-management, remove impediments (blockers), facilitate the events, protect focus.
  • The Product Owner — help with backlog management and clear goals, arrange stakeholder collaboration.
  • The organisation — help everyone understand and adopt Scrum, remove barriers between teams and stakeholders.
Everyday analogy Like a sports coach, not the team captain and not a player. They don't score the goals; they train the players, study the opponents, clear obstacles, and help the team get better every game.
★
Say this "My job is to make myself less needed over time. A great Scrum Master builds a team that can eventually run Scrum well without them."
Q9

Scrum Master vs Project Manager — what's the difference?

This is a classic trap for people transitioning into the role. The key: a Scrum Master has no command authority over the team.

★
Say this "A PM pushes work down and manages the plan. A Scrum Master creates the conditions for a self-managing team to succeed — leadership through influence, not authority."
Q10

What does the Product Owner do?

The PO is a single person accountable for maximising the value the team delivers. They are the voice of the customer and the decider on priorities. Their main tools are the Product Backlog and a clear Product Goal.

They decide what gets built and in what order — but not how, and not how much the team commits to in a Sprint.

★
Key point "One person, not a committee. For the PO to be effective, the organisation must respect their decisions."
Q11

What are Developers responsible for?

"Developers" in Scrum means anyone doing the work to create the increment — not just programmers. That includes testers, designers, analysts, and so on. They are responsible for:

  • Creating the Sprint Backlog (the plan for the Sprint).
  • Building a "Done" increment that meets the Definition of Done.
  • Adapting their plan each day toward the Sprint Goal.
  • Holding each other accountable as professionals.
Q12

What does "servant leadership" mean?

It's leading by serving the needs of the team first. Instead of "What do I want the team to do?", the servant leader asks "What does the team need to succeed, and how do I remove what's in the way?" The 2020 Scrum Guide phrases this as being a true leader who serves.

In practice: clearing blockers, shielding the team from distraction, asking questions instead of giving orders, and building the team's confidence to make its own decisions.

03 The Five Events

The Sprint and its four ceremonies

Every event is a formal opportunity to inspect and adapt. Each is timeboxed — given a maximum length so it stays focused.

The Sprint is itself an event — a container holding the other four.

Q13

What are the five Scrum events?

  1. The Sprint — the container for all other events (1–4 weeks).
  2. Sprint Planning — plan the upcoming Sprint (max 8 hrs for a 1-month Sprint).
  3. Daily Scrum — 15-min daily sync for the Developers.
  4. Sprint Review — show the increment to stakeholders and get feedback (max 4 hrs).
  5. Sprint Retrospective — the team improves how it works (max 3 hrs).
Q14

What happens in Sprint Planning?

The whole team plans the Sprint by answering three questions:

  • Why is this Sprint valuable? → they craft the Sprint Goal.
  • What can be done this Sprint? → they pick backlog items.
  • How will the work get done? → they break items into a plan.

The output is the Sprint Backlog (the Sprint Goal + selected items + the plan).

★
Watch out The PO proposes the "why/what," but the Developers decide how much they can realistically take on. Management can't stuff the Sprint.
Q15

What is the Daily Scrum (stand-up)?

A 15-minute, same-time-same-place daily meeting for the Developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal and re-plan the day. It keeps everyone aligned and surfaces blockers early.

The old "three questions" (What did I do? What will I do? Any blockers?) are optional — the 2020 Guide only says the Developers can structure it however they like, as long as it focuses on the Sprint Goal.

▲
Common trap It is not a status report to the Scrum Master or manager. It's the team talking to each other. The Scrum Master ensures it happens but doesn't run it.
Q16

What is the Sprint Review?

At the end of the Sprint, the team shows the working increment to stakeholders, gathers feedback, and discusses what to do next. It's a working session, not a one-way demo — the backlog often gets adjusted based on what's learned.

Everyday analogy Like showing a rough cut of a film to a test audience — you watch their reaction and decide what to change before the next round, rather than guessing alone.
Q17

What is the Sprint Retrospective — and why does it matter most?

The Retro is the team's private meeting to inspect how they worked (people, relationships, process, tools) and pick concrete improvements for next Sprint. The Review is about the product; the Retro is about the team.

Many practitioners call it the most important event because it's the engine of continuous improvement — the one place the team gets better at everything else.

★
Say this "A good retro ends with 1–2 specific, actionable improvements the team owns — not a long wish-list that gets forgotten."
Q18

What is timeboxing, and why use it?

Timeboxing means giving an activity a fixed maximum duration. When time's up, you stop. It creates focus, prevents endless meetings, and forces the team to prioritise.

Note the events have a maximum, not a required length — if the Daily Scrum takes 8 minutes, that's fine.

04 Artifacts & Commitments

The three things Scrum makes visible

Artifacts represent work or value and exist to maximise transparency. Each has a "commitment" attached to it that gives it focus.

Q19

What are the three Scrum artifacts?

  • Product Backlog — the single, ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product. Commitment: the Product Goal.
  • Sprint Backlog — the Sprint Goal plus the items chosen for this Sprint plus the plan to deliver them. Commitment: the Sprint Goal.
  • Increment — a usable, "Done" step toward the Product Goal. Commitment: the Definition of Done.
Q20

What is the Product Backlog, and who owns it?

It's the single source of truth for what the team might build — features, fixes, improvements — kept in priority order, most valuable at the top. The Product Owner owns it and decides the ordering.

Keeping it healthy is called backlog refinement (or grooming): breaking big items down, adding detail, and re-ordering. It's an ongoing activity, not a formal event.

Q21

What is the Sprint Backlog, and who owns it?

It's the Developers' plan for the current Sprint: the Sprint Goal, the items they selected, and how they intend to build them. It's owned entirely by the Developers, and they update it throughout the Sprint as they learn.

Q22

What is the "Definition of Done"?

A shared checklist of quality that a piece of work must meet to be considered truly finished — e.g. "code reviewed, tested, documented, deployed to staging." If it doesn't meet the DoD, it is not Done and can't be part of the increment.

Everyday analogy Like a restaurant's standard for "ready to serve": plated, correct temperature, garnished, checked. A half-cooked dish isn't "done" just because the timer went off.
★
Why it matters The DoD creates transparency and prevents "90% done" work that secretly isn't shippable. It's how Scrum protects quality.
Q23

What is a user story, and what makes a good one (INVEST)?

A user story is a short, plain-language description of a feature from the user's point of view. The common template:

"As a [type of user], I want [some goal] so that [some benefit]."

Good stories follow INVEST:

  • Independent · Negotiable · Valuable · Estimable · Small · Testable
★
Note "User story" isn't a Scrum term — it comes from XP — but Scrum teams use it heavily to fill the backlog.
Q24

What are story points?

A relative measure of effort/complexity/uncertainty — not hours. Teams estimate how big an item is compared to others, often using a Fibonacci-like scale (1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13…) and a game called Planning Poker.

Everyday analogy You don't know exactly how many minutes it takes to climb a hill, but you can confidently say "that hill is about twice as hard as this one." Points capture relative size, which turns out to be easier and more reliable to estimate than exact time.
05 Metrics & Tracking

How progress is made visible

Scrum itself mandates no metrics, but teams commonly use a few. Know what each shows — and its limits.

Q25

What is velocity?

Velocity is the average amount of work (usually story points) a team completes per Sprint. It's used for forecasting — roughly how many Sprints a chunk of backlog might take.

▲
Common trap Velocity is a planning aid for the team, never a productivity target or a way to compare teams. Two teams' points aren't comparable, and pushing for "higher velocity" just inflates estimates.
Q26

What is a burndown chart? (and burn-up)

A burndown chart tracks work remaining over time. The line should trend down toward zero by the end of the Sprint. Comparing the real line to the ideal line shows whether you're ahead or behind.

Above the ideal line = behind; below = ahead. It's a conversation-starter, not a scoreboard.

A burn-up chart is the mirror image — it tracks work completed rising toward a total, which makes scope changes easier to see.

Q27

How do you measure whether a Scrum team is successful?

Not by velocity or "story points delivered." The real measures are outcome-based:

  • Value delivered to customers and the business.
  • Predictability — does the team reliably meet its Sprint Goals?
  • Quality — few escaped defects, low technical debt.
  • Team health & happiness — sustainable pace, engaged people.
  • Continuous improvement — is the team getting better each Sprint?
★
Say this "I care more about outcomes than output. A team producing lots of features nobody uses isn't succeeding."
06 Scenario & Behavioral

"What would you do if…"

These test judgment, not memory. The winning pattern is almost always: don't command — coach, facilitate, and make the problem visible to the team.

Q28

A team member dominates every Daily Scrum. What do you do?

Approach: Observe first, then coach — don't publicly scold anyone.

  • Have a quiet 1-on-1 to raise it gently; they may not realise.
  • Reinforce the purpose: the Daily is for the whole team's alignment.
  • Introduce a light structure (e.g. walk the board, timebox each person, "take it offline" for deep dives).
  • If needed, raise it in the retro as a team topic so the team sets its own norm.
Q29

The Product Owner keeps changing priorities mid-Sprint. How do you handle it?

Approach: Protect the Sprint Goal — that's a core Scrum Master duty.

  • Coach the PO on why a stable Sprint Goal matters: constant churn kills focus and delivery.
  • Explain the escape valve: if priorities truly changed, they can be reflected in the next Sprint, or in the extreme the Sprint can be cancelled (only the PO can do that).
  • Channel new ideas into the Product Backlog for future refinement, not into the running Sprint.
★
Say this "I'd help the PO see that stability within a Sprint and flexibility between Sprints aren't in conflict — that's exactly what the Sprint boundary is for."
Q30

The team consistently fails to finish what it commits to. What now?

Approach: Get curious about the root cause — this is a retro topic, not a blame exercise.

  • Are they over-committing? Coach realistic forecasting using past data.
  • Are estimates poor? Improve refinement so items are smaller and clearer.
  • Are there hidden blockers, interruptions, or unplanned work? Make them visible and remove them.
  • Is the Definition of Done too vague? Tighten it.
★
Framing "Repeatedly missing the goal is a signal, not a failure. My job is to help the team inspect why and adapt — maybe they just need to commit to less and build trust in delivering it."
Q31

How do you handle conflict within the team?

Healthy conflict about ideas is good; personal conflict needs care. Facilitate a conversation, keep it focused on the work and shared goals, listen to all sides, and help them reach their own resolution rather than imposing one. Model the Scrum values of respect and openness. Escalate to management only if it becomes a serious HR issue.

Q32

Stakeholders keep assigning work directly to Developers, bypassing the PO. What do you do?

This undermines both the PO and the Sprint. As Scrum Master serving the organisation:

  • Coach stakeholders on how Scrum works — all work flows through the Product Backlog and the PO.
  • Empower the Developers to redirect such requests to the PO politely.
  • Make the impact visible: side-work steals capacity from the agreed Sprint Goal.
Q33

The team is disengaged and quiet in retrospectives. How do you fix it?

Usually a sign of low psychological safety or retro fatigue. Try: varying the format (Start/Stop/Continue, Sailboat, Mad-Sad-Glad), making it safe to be honest, ensuring past action items actually got done (nothing kills a retro faster than "nothing ever changes"), and occasionally stepping out so the team can speak freely.

★
Key insight "If people don't see change come out of retros, they stop investing in them. The fix is following through on improvements, visibly."
07 Scaling & Advanced

Beyond a single team

Mid-to-senior interviews probe how Scrum stretches across many teams, and how it compares to its cousin, Kanban.

Q34

How does Scrum scale to many teams? (SAFe, LeSS, Nexus, Scrum of Scrums)

When one product needs several teams, you use a scaling framework to coordinate them:

  • Scrum of Scrums — a simple sync meeting where a rep from each team coordinates dependencies.
  • Nexus — Scrum.org's lightweight framework for ~3–9 teams on one product; adds a "Nexus Integration Team."
  • LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum) — keeps Scrum as-is with one Product Backlog and one PO across many teams; minimal extra process.
  • SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework) — the heaviest and most prescriptive; adds portfolio/program layers. Popular in large enterprises, sometimes criticised as not truly agile.
★
Say this "The principle across all of them is the same: keep teams aligned to one product goal while managing cross-team dependencies. I'd pick the lightest framework that solves the actual problem."
Q35

Scrum vs Kanban — and what's Scrumban?

Scrum works in fixed cadences with defined roles; Kanban is a continuous flow that limits work-in-progress and pulls new work as capacity frees up. Scrumban blends them — a Scrum structure with Kanban's WIP limits and flow, popular for maintenance/support teams.

Q36

What is technical debt, and how should a Scrum Master handle it?

Technical debt is the future cost of taking shortcuts today — messy or quick code that will slow the team later, like interest on a loan. The Scrum Master helps the team keep it visible (make it backlog items), coaches a strong Definition of Done to prevent new debt, and helps the PO understand why some capacity each Sprint should go to paying it down.

Q37

Can one person be Scrum Master for several teams? Can the role be part-time?

Yes — Scrum doesn't forbid it, and experienced Scrum Masters often serve 2–3 teams. But it depends on team maturity: a new team forming needs far more attention than a mature, self-managing one. The risk is spreading too thin and becoming a passive "meeting scheduler" rather than an active coach.

★
Nuance to show "It's possible, but I'd push back if I'm assigned so many teams that I can only book meetings, not actually coach. That's a red flag for the org's understanding of the role."
08 Curveballs & Traps

The questions designed to catch you out

Interviewers slip these in to separate the memorisers from the people who truly understand Scrum. Each one has a tempting wrong answer.

Q38

Isn't the Daily Scrum a status meeting for the Scrum Master?

▲
The trap Saying "yes, everyone reports their status to me."

Correct answer: No. The Daily Scrum is by and for the Developers to re-plan their day toward the Sprint Goal. The Scrum Master ensures it happens and is effective, but doesn't run it or collect status. It's a peer conversation, not a report-up.

Q39

Who writes the user stories — only the Product Owner?

▲
The trap "The Product Owner writes all the stories."

Correct answer: The PO is accountable for the Product Backlog and its ordering, but anyone on the team can write or contribute to backlog items and stories. In fact, collaboration on stories (with Developers who understand the technical side) produces much better ones. Accountability ≠ doing all the work.

Q40

Can a Sprint be extended or cancelled?

▲
The trap "Sure, if the team needs a few more days, just extend the Sprint."

Correct answer: A Sprint's length is never extended — that would break the steady cadence and the whole point of a timebox. If work isn't done, it goes back to the Product Backlog for a future Sprint.

A Sprint can be cancelled — but only by the Product Owner, and only if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete (e.g. a major business pivot). This is rare.

One last tip for the interview itself.
When you get a "what would you do" question, narrate your thinking, not just an answer: acknowledge the situation → say you'd get curious about the root cause → describe coaching/facilitating rather than commanding → mention making it visible or bringing it to the right event. That structure signals a real Scrum Master mindset, and it works even for scenarios you've never seen before.
